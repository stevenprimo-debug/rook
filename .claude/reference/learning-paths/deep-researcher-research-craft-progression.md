# Research Craft — Progression Path

## Who This Is For

The operator who needs to make decisions on real evidence — competitive briefs, market scans, technical due diligence, pre-meeting prep, trademark / name checks, tool and MCP discovery — and refuses to confuse "I read three blog posts" with "I did the research." Founders, agency principals, marketers, and analysts who own the intel that informs spend, hire, build, and pivot decisions.

By the end of this path you should be able to:
- Evaluate sources by tier (primary, secondary, tertiary).
- Synthesize across sources without re-arranging one source.
- Calibrate your own confidence honestly.
- Produce a brief that informs a named decision.
- Run a prediction log and improve calibration over time.
- Publish research that other practitioners cite.

## Stage 1: Foundations (weeks 1-3)

**Goal:** Internalize source-tier hierarchy. Learn to read primary sources. Develop confidence-level discipline.

**Read / Watch:**
- *How to Read a Book* — Mortimer Adler and Charles Van Doren. The four levels of reading; the analytical-reading discipline is the spine of research craft.
- *The Craft of Research* — Wayne Booth, Gregory Colomb, Joseph Williams. The University of Chicago research-writing primer; the most accessible serious research methodology book in print.
- *Bad Science* — Ben Goldacre. The pop-science critique of evidence misuse; teaches the smell test by negative example.
- *Calling Bullshit* — Carl Bergstrom and Jevin West. Modern data-literacy primer; the chapters on selection bias and base rates are mandatory.
- **The University of Washington "Calling Bullshit" lecture series** (callingbullshit.org) — free; the full course; the most useful research-literacy training available online.
- *Superforecasting* — Philip Tetlock and Dan Gardner. The Good Judgment Project's findings on confidence calibration.

**Practice:**
- Build a source-tier hierarchy: primary (filings, datasets, peer-reviewed, direct interviews), secondary (analyst reports, trade press, white papers), tertiary (blogs, social, AI summaries). Tag every source you read for one week by tier.
- Pick one company you respect. Read their last three years of 10-K filings (or equivalent for non-US / private companies — annual reports, S-1, press releases). Take notes only on primary sources. The exercise teaches what primary actually looks like.
- Develop a confidence-level taxonomy for your own claims: verified (multiple primary sources agree), working hypothesis (one strong source, plausibility-check ok), speculative (interesting but unsupported). Use the taxonomy in every brief.
- Write a one-page research brief on any topic. The brief must name the decision it enables. If no decision, no brief.

**Skill check:**
- Hand someone a research brief you wrote. They can identify (a) the decision it enables, (b) the three highest-tier sources cited, and (c) the confidence level of every major claim.
- The brief is not a list of links.
- You can defend each major claim with a named source you can produce on demand.
- Your confidence-level taxonomy has fired on at least one claim you would have stated more strongly without it.

## Stage 2: Applied Practice (weeks 4-10)

**Goal:** Synthesize across sources. Run a competitive intelligence cycle. Build a personal research operating system.

**Read / Watch:**
- *The Investigative Reporter's Handbook* — IRE (Investigative Reporters and Editors). The journalism-craft manual; the chapters on document tracing and FOIA requests carry to all professional research.
- *Where Good Ideas Come From* — Steven Johnson. The "slow hunch" framework; research synthesis is the management of multiple slow hunches.
- *Range* — David Epstein. The case for breadth-then-synthesis; the kind/wicked-environment distinction frames what kind of research a question needs.
- **The Generalist** (readthegeneralist.com) — Mario Gabriele's research-driven business writing; study the structure of his pieces.
- **Substack research-writers worth subscribing to:** Stratechery (Ben Thompson), Not Boring (Packy McCormick), The Diff (Byrne Hobart), Doomberg (energy markets), The Generalist (Mario Gabriele).
- **Open-source intelligence resources** — Bellingcat's online investigation toolkit (bellingcat.com/resources). Free; the most concentrated OSINT training.

**Practice:**
- Run one competitive intelligence brief on a real competitor in your space. Use only primary and secondary sources. The brief includes: business model, unit economics (inferred or disclosed), distribution channels, recent moves, the three things they do better than you, the three things they don't.
- Build a personal research operating system: where you save raw clippings, how you tag them, how you synthesize across them, where the briefs live. Use Obsidian, Notion, or whatever fits — the system matters more than the tool.
- Run a pre-meeting brief cadence: for the next 10 important meetings, write a one-page brief before the meeting. Five-minute read. Includes who's in the room, what they care about, what they've done recently, the three questions you're prepared to ask. Compare brief to actual meeting; iterate the template.
- Develop a tool/MCP discovery loop: monthly scan of new tools in your domain. Three-question rubric: what does it do, who is it for, what does it replace. Add the keepers to your stack.

**Skill check:**
- A competitive brief you produced informed a real decision (build vs. buy, pivot vs. persevere, hire vs. contract).
- The decision-maker can point to the brief as the input.
- The brief was synthesized, not re-arranged.
- Your pre-meeting brief cadence has shown up to at least 10 meetings and improved meeting outcomes measurably.

## Stage 3: Advanced Mastery (months 3-9)

**Goal:** Develop forecasting calibration. Run multi-source synthesis on novel domains. Publish research that other practitioners cite.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Superforecasting* — Philip Tetlock and Dan Gardner. Re-read with discipline.
- *Thinking, Fast and Slow* — Daniel Kahneman. System 1 / System 2 distinction; the cognitive-bias catalog matters.
- *Noise* — Daniel Kahneman, Olivier Sibony, Cass Sunstein. Variability in judgment as the underrated source of error.
- *The Signal and the Noise* — Nate Silver. Forecasting under uncertainty.
- *How to Measure Anything* — Douglas Hubbard. The Fermi-estimation discipline; the "anything can be measured if it matters" argument.
- *The Field Guide to Understanding Human Error* — Sidney Dekker. The local-rationality lens that prevents you from misreading other people's decisions.
- *The Origins of Political Order* — Francis Fukuyama. The model for how to do synthesis across a vast literature; useful as exemplar of long-form research craft.
- **The Good Judgment Project's superforecasting platform** (goodjudgment.com) — gated; play the prediction-tournament if you can.
- *Skin in the Game* — Nassim Taleb. The asymmetry-detection lens applied to expertise claims.
- **The Diff by Byrne Hobart** (paid; thediff.co) — most rigorous business-research writing on Substack.
- **Stratechery** (Ben Thompson) — daily; the strategic-synthesis discipline.

**Practice:**
- Make 10 dated, written predictions about your domain in a research-prediction log. Score them at 90 / 180 / 365 days. Track your calibration (when you say 70%, are you right 70% of the time?).
- Run a synthesis project on a novel domain — something genuinely outside your existing knowledge. Read 30+ sources. Produce a 10-page synthesis. The synthesis surfaces a pattern not explicit in any single source.
- Publish one research piece publicly (blog, newsletter, Twitter / X thread). Cite primary sources. Invite critique. Iterate.
- Develop a "what would change my mind" discipline. Before every major claim in a brief, write the evidence that would disprove the claim. Look for that evidence. Update.

**Skill check:**
- Your prediction log shows calibration improving over time.
- A research piece you published has been cited by another practitioner.
- You can name three claims you held last quarter and updated this quarter based on new evidence — not on social pressure.
- Your synthesis project produced a pattern not explicit in any single source.
- Your "what would change my mind" discipline has fired on at least one major claim.

**Quarterly cadence:**
- Re-score your prediction log. Review one full brief from last quarter against what actually happened.
- Write the meta-brief: what did your research method get right, what did it get wrong, what changes for next quarter.
- Audit your source-tier hierarchy: which sources actually informed decisions; which were noise; remove the noise from the standing rotation.
- Re-read one foundational research-craft text (Booth/Colomb/Williams, Tetlock, Kahneman, Hubbard); identify what you can use now.
