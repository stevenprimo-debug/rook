# Visual Storyteller — Progression Path

## Who This Is For

The operator who designs the surfaces customers see — landing pages, decks, proposals, dashboards, brand systems, packaging — and refuses to accept "professionally competent but quietly off" work. Solo founders, marketers, agency operators, and product owners who do their own visual work or supervise contractors.

By the end of this path you should be able to:
- Read a typeface by its bones.
- Build a palette without color-picker tools.
- Set type to a baseline grid.
- Animate with restraint.
- Look at a finished surface and name the one move that would make it 30% better.
- Critique designs in named terms instead of "it looks off."

## Stage 1: Foundations (weeks 1-3)

**Goal:** Read type and color fluently. Build hierarchy that works without color. Critique work in named terms instead of "it just looks off."

**Read / Watch:**
- *The Elements of Typographic Style* — Robert Bringhurst. Read chapters 1-5 first; the rest are reference.
- *Thinking with Type* — Ellen Lupton. The accessible companion to Bringhurst.
- *Interaction of Color* — Josef Albers. Color is relational, not absolute.
- *Grid Systems in Graphic Design* — Josef Müller-Brockmann. Swiss-grid bible.
- **The Futur YouTube channel** — Chris Do. Free; uneven; the design-critique episodes are the keepers.
- **Practical Typography** by Matthew Butterick (practicaltypography.com). Free, web-native, ruthlessly opinionated.
- *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information* — Edward Tufte. The information-design canon.
- *Universal Principles of Design* — William Lidwell et al. 125 named principles in one volume.
- *A Type Primer* — John Kane. The teaching version; the exercises are the keeper.
- **Letterform Archive's online exhibits** (letterformarchive.org). Free; the type-history education the field needs.

**Practice:**
- Type-pair drill: pick five pairs (headline + body) you respect from books, magazines, sites. Identify each face by name. If you cannot name it, learn it via WhatTheFont or Identifont.
- Build five hierarchies for the same content using ONLY type weight and size — no color. The exercise teaches that color is the last variable, not the first.
- Hand-letter the alphabet in three different historical styles (humanist, geometric, slab). Once. The kinesthetic memory makes typeface selection faster forever.
- Critique five landing pages in writing. Use the language from Bringhurst: leading, tracking, x-height, color relationships, grid violations. No "it looks off."

**Skill check:**
- You can look at a typeface and name its category (humanist sans, geometric sans, transitional serif, slab, display) inside three seconds.
- You can describe a palette in terms of hue / saturation / value relationships, not "blue and orange."
- You can identify a baseline-grid violation from a screenshot without a ruler.
- You can articulate why a design works in terms of contrast, rhythm, hierarchy, and proximity.

## Stage 2: Applied Practice (weeks 4-10)

**Goal:** Ship a brand system. Design with motion. Build a personal taste library.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Designing Brand Identity* — Alina Wheeler.
- *How to* — Michael Bierut. The Pentagram partner's working monograph.
- *Logo Modernism* — Jens Müller. Identity work 1940-1980.
- **Refactoring UI** — Adam Wathan and the operator Schoger.
- **Stripe Press books** broadly — beautifully typeset; study them as objects.
- **Femke van Schoonhoven's YouTube channel** — product design walkthroughs.
- *The Design of Everyday Things* — Don Norman. The product-design canon.
- *Make It Stick* by Tobias Frere-Jones — typography talks; YouTube and Frere-Jones type podcasts.
- *Anatomy of Type* — Stephen Coles. Type-classification reference.
- The vendored reference clipping `context/references/50-modern-fonts.md` — internalize the working type palette.

**Practice:**
- Build a complete brand system for a fictional or real product: wordmark, lockup, palette, type system, three layout templates, one motion principle. Document in a brand guidelines deck.
- Set a long article (1,500+ words) to a baseline grid. Every element snaps. The discipline is invisible to readers and felt as quality.
- Design the same hero section three ways: Swiss-grid, editorial-magazine, contemporary-tech. The exercise teaches that "good design" is plural.
- Animate one micro-interaction (button hover, modal open, page transition) using CSS or Framer. Then animate it three more times with different easing curves. Pick the one that earns its motion.
- Build a personal taste library on **Are.na** or **Eagle** or **Mymind**. 200 references, tagged. The library is the design system before any project starts.

**Skill check:**
- Your shipped brand system survives three weeks of use without you wanting to change anything fundamental.
- The motion choices feel right, not loud.
- You can defend each type and color decision in named terms — not "it felt right."
- Your taste library has cleared 200 references and is tagged by theme.

## Stage 3: Advanced Mastery (months 3-9)

**Goal:** Develop a recognizable point of view. Design at production-quality without templates. Critique work in the language other senior designers use.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Eye Magazine* archive — long-form design writing; the visual journalism of the field.
- *Idea Magazine* — Japanese typography and design quarterly; the visual diet matters.
- *Slanted Magazine* — current European editorial design.
- **Working Not Working** and **Dribbble** — observe but do not imitate; the algorithm rewards a particular aesthetic that ages fast.
- **Brand New** (underconsideration.com/brandnew) — the daily identity-launch review blog; the comment section is half the value.
- **Femke / The Futur / DesignCourse / Flux Academy** YouTube channels — varied opinions; pick one whose taste you trust and ignore the rest.
- *About Face* — Alan Cooper. The product-design foundational text.
- *Don't Make Me Think* — the operator Krug. UX heuristics; short; mandatory.

**Practice:**
- Redesign a competitor's surface (with attribution) — landing page, app screen, proposal cover. Annotate every decision change with the reason. Publish the case study.
- Run a peer design crit monthly with 2-3 designers whose taste you respect. Bring work; receive critique without defending; revise based on it.
- Develop a personal style guide: three typefaces you reach for and why, three palettes that are yours, three motion patterns you trust. The constraint is the freedom.
- Ship one piece of work to a real audience (client, product launch, conference talk slide deck). Real stakes accelerate the learning curve more than any class.

**Skill check:**
- A peer designer can identify your work in a lineup of three anonymous samples.
- You have a point of view, not just craft.
- You can defend a design decision in a real client critique without folding.
- Your case studies attract design-conscious clients without you describing the work as "modern" or "clean."

## Ongoing Development

**Stay current:**
- **Brand New** (underconsideration.com/brandnew) — daily.
- **Sidebar.io** — five design links per day; curated.
- **Refactoring UI newsletter** and the Tailwind UI launches — practical product-design pulse.
- **Working Not Working** weekly newsletter — global design industry pulse.
- **Eye Magazine** — quarterly long-form.
- **Idea Magazine** — quarterly Japanese.
- **Slanted** — quarterly European.
- **Print Magazine** archive — historical visual design.
- **It's Nice That** — daily; UK-based design and culture.
- **Awwwards** — for the contemporary web-design pulse; observe critically.
- **Smashing Magazine** — for the web-design depth.
- **Type Network and Commercial Type** — type-foundry release notes.
- **The Brand Identity** newsletter — weekly; identity-launch reviews.
- **Refactoring UI's email list** — product-design practical tips.
- **The Design Buddies Discord** — variable but useful for craft questions.
- **NN/g (Nielsen Norman Group)** — UX research foundational essays.
- **Mockup.io and SiteInspire** — daily-feed inspiration archives.
- **Brand New Conference** annual sessions — paid but high signal.
- **Layers conference recordings** — Figma's annual; free.
- **UX Tools annual survey** — Taylor Palmer; what designers are actually using.
- **The Type Drawers forums** — for type-design depth.
- **Designer News and Hacker News (design tag)** — daily.

**Communities to join:**
- **AIGA local chapter** — in-person matters; portfolio reviews are the highest-leverage event.
- **Friends of Figma** community — Figma has the deepest community currently.
- **Dribbble Pro / Designer Hangout** Slack — variable but useful for tool talk.
- **Layers conference and Config conference** (annual) — Figma and adjacent.
- **The Type Directors Club** for typography depth.
- A small peer crit group (3-5 designers) — monthly; structured critique.

**Quarterly cadence:**
- Print your last quarter's work. Pin it to a wall. Sit for two hours. Identify the one move that would have made each piece 30% better. Write it down.
- Re-audit your taste library. Prune what no longer earns its keep; tag what's still useful.
- Re-read one foundational book (Bringhurst, Albers, Müller-Brockmann); identify what you can use now that you couldn't six months ago.
