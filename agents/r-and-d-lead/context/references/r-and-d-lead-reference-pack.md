## Source 1: The Lean Startup — Principles (theleanstartup.com)

**Author/Publisher:** Eric Ries / theleanstartup.com (official site)
**URL:** https://theleanstartup.com/principles
**Domain teaching:** Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop; validated learning as the unit of progress; MVP definition; Innovation Accounting; the pivot vs. persevere decision
**Why authoritative:** This is the canonical primary-source articulation of the five Lean Startup principles, including the formal definitions of Build-Measure-Learn, validated learning, and MVP — written and published by Eric Ries on his own official site. It establishes the foundational vocabulary that all downstream lean experimentation literature inherits.

**Key concepts extracted:**
- **Build-Measure-Learn**: "The fundamental activity of a startup is to turn ideas into products, measure how customers respond, and then learn whether to pivot or persevere. All successful startup processes should be geared to accelerate that feedback loop."
- **Validated learning**: "The unit of progress for Lean Startups is validated learning — a rigorous method for demonstrating progress when one is embedded in the soil of extreme uncertainty."
- **MVP**: Develop the minimum viable product to begin learning; instrument with actionable metrics (not vanity metrics); use the Five Whys for root-cause discipline.
- **Innovation Accounting**: Measurement framework that holds experimental work accountable to meaningful milestones rather than build-output metrics.

**Kill-criterion relevance:** The pivot-or-persevere decision is the Lean Startup's native kill criterion. The principles page frames this as a deliberate, metric-driven gate — not a deadline extension. "Should this product be built?" precedes "Can this product be built?"

## Source 2: the operator Blank — "An MVP Is Not a Cheaper Product, It's About Smart Learning"

**Author/Publisher:** the operator Blank / steveblank.com
**URL:** https://steveblank.com/2013/07/22/an-mvp-is-not-a-cheaper-product-its-about-smart-learning/
**Domain teaching:** MVP as a learning instrument, not a feature-reduced product; kill-criterion framing; cheap-hack experiments before expensive prototypes; what constitutes sufficient signal
**Why authoritative:** the operator Blank is the originator of the Customer Development framework and the intellectual foundation of the Lean Startup movement. This post is his most direct treatment of the MVP-as-learning-tool concept and directly addresses the anti-pattern of over-engineering validation experiments. Published on his primary blog.

**Key concepts extracted:**
- **The cheap-hack MVP**: An agricultural-drone startup was counseled to rent a plane, buy flight time, and hand-process hyper-spectral images rather than build UAV software — same learning at 1/10th the cost in 1–2 days.
- **Kill-criterion signal strength**: Blank (via commenter Erik Larson) provides explicit thresholds: B2B signal visible in 3–7 customers; consumer signal in 200–1,000 users. "Strong negative: No one is excited about the concept."
- **Anti-pattern diagnosis**: Founders over-engineer MVPs because of six identifiable psychological biases (fear of discovery, engineering identity, belief in idea infallibility). Naming these is itself a kill-criterion discipline tool.
- **"Great founders keep their eye on the prize"**: The prize is validated learning about whether customers care about the outcome — not validated learning about whether the technology works.

**Sliding-time-box relevance:** The post implicitly defines the anti-pattern: when the engineering MVP is treated as the experiment, the experiment grows to fill all available time. Blank's reframe ("what cheap hack tests the same hypothesis?") is the structural counter.

## Source 3: the operator Blank — The Four Steps to the Epiphany (Stanford course PDF)

**Author/Publisher:** Steven G. Blank / Stanford University course handout
**URL:** https://web.stanford.edu/group/e145/cgi-bin/winter/drupal/upload/handouts/Four_Steps.pdf
**Domain teaching:** Customer Development as a parallel process to product development; the four-step model (Customer Discovery → Customer Validation → Customer Creation → Company Building); kill criteria embedded as phase-exit gates; earlyvangelists; market-type taxonomy
**Why authoritative:** This is the foundational 2005 text that launched the Customer Development methodology and, by Eric Ries's own account, the Lean Startup movement. The Stanford PDF is the original course handout distributed in Blank's E145 class. It contains the complete Customer Development checklist (Appendix B) — the field-tested, 50+ item kill-criterion framework for each phase exit.

**Key concepts extracted:**
- **Customer Discovery**: Turns the founder's vision into testable business-model hypotheses. Phase exit gate: confirmed that the problem matters and customers will pay.
- **Customer Validation**: Builds a repeatable sales roadmap. Phase exit gate: a proven, repeatable, scalable sales process — if not found, return to Discovery (explicit kill/pivot signal).
- **Earlyvangelist criteria** (five-part kill framework for early customer qualification):
  1. Has a problem
  2. Knows they have the problem
  3. Actively searching for a solution with a timetable
  4. Has cobbled together an interim solution (problem is painful enough)
  5. Has or can acquire budget
- **The 10 flaws of the product development model**: Documents why premature execution — running launch activities before validation — creates the "death spiral" (fire-hire cycles, sunk costs, no exit criteria).
- **Market-type taxonomy**: Existing, New, Re-segmented (low-cost), Re-segmented (niche). Each requires different time horizons and different kill thresholds — a new market can take two years to show traction; an existing market will fail fast.

**Kill-criterion relevance:** The Customer Development Checklist (Appendix B, pp. 181–230) is the most operationally detailed kill/proceed framework in the entire lean canon.

## Source 4: the operator Blank — "Why Build, Measure, Learn Isn't Just Throwing Things Against the Wall"

**Author/Publisher:** the operator Blank / steveblank.com
**URL:** https://steveblank.com/2015/05/06/build-measure-learn-throw-things-against-the-wall-and-see-if-they-work/
**Domain teaching:** Correcting the Build-Measure-Learn misreading; hypothesis-first discipline; learning-velocity framing; graduation from prototype to production
**Why authoritative:** Written by Blank as a direct rebuttal to the most common Lean Startup misapplication — treating BML as "ship fast and see what sticks." Published on his primary blog in 2015, this post provides the corrective frame for the sliding-time-box and novelty-chasing anti-patterns.

**Key concepts extracted:**
- **Hypotheses → Experiments → Tests → Insights**: Blank proposes replacing the BML label with this explicit sequence to prevent "fire-ready-aim" execution.
- **"Build" is the wrong starting word**: Startups do not start with ideas or products — they start with *hypotheses* (educated guesses about the business model). The Business Model Canvas is the tool for making hypotheses explicit before any building occurs.
- **Learning-velocity metric**: The goal of each MVP is not to impress customers but to gain *insight* that advances the founder's vision. Low-fidelity MVPs (slides, wireframes, clay models) advance to higher fidelity as learning accumulates. Fidelity escalation is a graduation gate.
- **The "Version 3 Rule"**: Historically, products required three versions to be right — because Version 1 lacked customer feedback loops. BML is the mechanism that collapses the Version 1→3 cycle.
- **Customer Development as scientific method**: The post explicitly frames the process as analogous to scientific hypothesis testing, making "learning that invalidates a hypothesis" a positive outcome rather than a failure.

**Anti-sliding-time-box relevance:** The reframe from "build" to "hypothesize" creates a natural forcing function — an experiment without a pre-declared hypothesis and success criterion is not a valid experiment. This operationally blocks the "just keep iterating" pattern.

## Source 5: Pretotyping.org — Alberto Savoia's Official Site

**Author/Publisher:** Alberto Savoia (former Engineering Director, Google) / pretotyping.org
**URL:** https://www.pretotyping.org
**Domain teaching:** Pretotyping as a discipline cheaper than prototyping; the Law of Market Failure; YODA (Your Own DAta); seven strategies for fighting market failure; testing desirability before feasibility
**Why authoritative:** Alberto Savoia coined "pretotyping" while at Google in 2010. This is the official site, featuring his book *The Right It*, his Stanford Entrepreneurial Thought Leaders Seminar presentation, and the complete seven-strategy framework. It is the canonical source for the concept of testing *desirability* before *feasibility* — the key distinction between pretotyping and prototyping.

**Key concepts extracted:**
- **Law of Market Failure**: "Most new ideas fail in the market even if they are competently executed." The number is not bad execution — it is building the wrong thing.
- **Pretotyping vs. Prototyping**: Prototyping answers "Can we build it?" (feasibility). Pretotyping answers "Should we build it?" (desirability). Pretotyping comes *before* prototyping.
- **YODA (Your Own DAta)**: Reject anecdote, survey, and focus-group data. Insist on data from real market behavior — even if the "product" is a facade (a Mechanical Turk backend, a landing page, a paper mock-up).
- **"Think global, test local"**: Validate the idea in the smallest, cheapest local proxy before investing in broad rollout. This is the structural kill-criterion: if the local test fails, stop.
- **Validation speed**: Pretotyping can be executed in hours to days, not weeks. This is a hard discipline: if a validation experiment takes longer than a few days to design and run, the team is building a prototype, not a pretotype.
- **Seven Strategies**: (1) Obey the Law of Market Failure; (2) Make sure you are building The Right It; (3) Don't get lost in Thoughtland; (4) Trust only in YODA; (5) Pretotype It; (6) Say it with numbers; (7) Think global, test local.

**Kill-criterion relevance:** Pretotyping is structurally a kill-criterion discipline — every pretotype is designed to generate a go/kill signal before prototype investment. The "Say it with numbers" principle requires pre-committing to a quantitative pass/fail threshold before running the test.

## Source 6: SVPG — "The Inconvenient Truth About Product" (Marty Cagan)

**Author/Publisher:** Marty Cagan / Silicon Valley Product Group (svpg.com)
**URL:** https://www.svpg.com/the-inconvenient-truth-about-product/
**Domain teaching:** Product discovery as the primary competency of strong teams; kill-criterion discipline ("at least half of ideas will not work"); graduation from discovery to delivery; weak vs. strong team behaviors
**Why authoritative:** Marty Cagan is the founding partner of SVPG and former VP Product at eBay, Netscape, and AOL. This article from 2013 is his most-cited treatment of why discovery-discipline — and the willingness to kill ideas — is the defining differentiator between strong and weak product organizations. SVPG is the premier practitioner voice for product management in Silicon Valley.

**Key concepts extracted:**
- **Two Inconvenient Truths**:
  1. At least half of all product ideas will not work.
  2. Even for ideas that prove valuable, it typically takes several iterations to deliver expected business value.
- **Strong teams vs. weak teams** (verbatim behavioral comparison):
  - Weak: Plow through the roadmap, blame stakeholders for failed features, schedule another roadmap iteration.
  - Strong: Quickly determine good ideas from bad; prototype and validate ideas with customers, engineers, and stakeholders in *days* rather than months.
- **Prototype-and-validate cadence**: Strong teams measure discovery speed in days. This is an operational kill-criterion for process health — if validation cycles are measured in weeks, the team is running the wrong process.
- **Graduation gate**: Discovery produces a validated solution that is usable, useful, and feasible. Only after all three criteria pass does the idea graduate to delivery.
- **The roadmap anti-pattern**: Roadmap-driven teams have no kill criterion. Items move forward by calendar date, not by evidence. This is the institutional form of the sliding-time-box anti-pattern.

**Learning-velocity relevance:** Cagan's explicit standard — "prototype and validate in days" — is the operational benchmark for learning velocity in a disciplined discovery practice.

## Source 7: Product Talk — "Opportunity Solution Trees" (Teresa Torres)

**Author/Publisher:** Teresa Torres / producttalk.org
**URL:** https://www.producttalk.org/2016/08/opportunity-solution-tree/
**Domain teaching:** Continuous discovery habits; the Opportunity Solution Tree (OST) as a structured discovery funnel; assumption testing before building; outcome-first orientation; preventing novelty-chasing
**Why authoritative:** Teresa Torres is the author of *Continuous Discovery Habits* (2021), endorsed by Marty Cagan, and the practitioner who codified the Opportunity Solution Tree framework. The OST is the most operationally detailed implementation of the Discovery Funnel concept (problem-solution-market-business fit gates) in current use. This article is the original, canonical description of the framework, published on her primary professional blog.

**Key concepts extracted:**
- **Opportunity Solution Tree structure**:
  - **Root**: Desired outcome (business need). All discovery is anchored to a measurable outcome, not a feature.
  - **Opportunity space**: Customer needs, pain points, and desires mapped from continuous interviews.
  - **Solution space**: Potential solutions to a *single, targeted* opportunity — prevents solution-first thinking.
  - **Assumption tests**: The riskiest assumptions for each solution candidate are identified and tested before building.
- **Discovery Funnel as kill criteria at each gate**:
  - Is there a measurable outcome to pursue? (Problem fit gate)
  - Are there real opportunities (customer needs) that drive the outcome? (Opportunity fit gate)
  - Does the proposed solution address the target opportunity? (Solution fit gate)
  - Do the riskiest assumptions hold? (Business fit gate — if not, discard and generate new solutions)
- **Anti-novelty-chasing discipline**: The OST constrains the team to one target opportunity at a time, explicitly to limit work-in-progress and prevent the pattern of chasing new problems before validating the current one.
- **Sliding-time-box countermeasure**: The tree stabilizes after 6–8 customer interviews. The framework creates a natural cadence (one interview per week, tree review every 3–4 interviews) — a structured time-box with explicit learning gates.
- **Outcome taxonomy**: Business outcomes, product outcomes, and traction metrics are distinguished, preventing teams from tracking output metrics (features shipped) as proxy for learning progress.
- **Real evidence of use**: Grailed reported a 20% lift in LTV using opportunity mapping; Seera Group discovered a new market using the framework during travel halts.

**Learning-velocity relevance:** The OST operationalizes learning velocity by making the rate of assumption tests — not the rate of feature delivery — the primary measure of discovery progress.

## Recommended reading order

1. **the operator Blank — Four Steps to the Epiphany (Stanford PDF)** — Start here for the foundational mental model: what a startup is, why it must search rather than execute, and the Customer Development phase-exit framework. Skim Appendix B (Customer Development Checklist) as a kill-criterion reference.

2. **theleanstartup.com/principles** — Read second to internalize the five Lean Startup principles. This is the shared vocabulary for all team communication about experimentation.

3. **the operator Blank — "Why Build, Measure, Learn Isn't Just Throwing Things Against the Wall"** — Read before designing any experiment. This post reframes BML from a shipping cadence to a hypothesis-testing discipline and operationally blocks the sliding-time-box anti-pattern.

4. **pretotyping.org** — Read before designing any MVP or prototype. Savoia's seven strategies establish the discipline of testing desirability before investing in feasibility. Use the "Say it with numbers" principle to pre-commit kill thresholds on every experiment.

5. **the operator Blank — "An MVP Is Not a Cheaper Product"** — Read alongside pretotyping.org. The drone case study is the best single illustration of the cheap-hack principle and provides explicit quantitative signal thresholds for B2B and consumer contexts.

6. **SVPG — "The Inconvenient Truth About Product"** — Read before any sprint planning. Cagan's two truths and the strong/weak team comparison are the executive-communication frame for why kill-criterion discipline is a competency, not a process.

7. **Product Talk — "Opportunity Solution Trees"** — Read last, as the integrating operational framework. The OST synthesizes Customer Development, continuous discovery, and kill-criterion discipline into a single visual tool a product trio can use in every sprint.
