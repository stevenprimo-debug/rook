# Experiment Discipline — Progression Path

## Who This Is For

The operator who runs experiments — prototypes, novel-stack probes, "what if" investigations — and refuses to let any of them silently become production work without earning it. Founders, R&D leads, technical co-founders, solo tinkerers, and anyone whose competitive advantage depends on learning velocity, not feature volume.

By the end of this path you should be able to:
- Design an experiment with a named kill criterion.
- Run a portfolio with most experiments killed early.
- Graduate the rare survivors with rigor.
- Produce learnings that compound across cycles.
- Develop "cheap teardown" discipline that protects against sunk-cost extension.
- Publish experimental write-ups including failures.

## Stage 1: Foundations (weeks 1-3)

**Goal:** Internalize kill-criteria. Run your first three-experiment portfolio with disciplined termination.

**Read / Watch:**
- *The Lean Startup* — Eric Ries. The build-measure-learn loop and the persevere-or-pivot decision. Read once for theory; pull out the "innovation accounting" chapters for craft.
- *Sprint* — Jake Knapp, John Zeratsky, Braden Kowitz. The five-day design sprint as the most concrete experiment-runbook in print.
- *Testing Business Ideas* — David Bland and Alex Osterwalder. 44 named experiment patterns; the closest thing to a periodic table for experiments.
- *Running Lean* — Ash Maurya. The Lean Canvas and the discipline of running experiments against a single canvas.
- **Y Combinator's Startup School** (startupschool.org) — free video lectures; the Paul Graham and Jessica Livingston content on experiment-discipline is canon.
- **Paul Graham's essays** (paulgraham.com) — "Do Things That Don't Scale," "Schlep Blindness," "The Hardest Lessons for Startups to Learn." Read all three.

**Practice:**
- Design three small experiments with explicit kill criteria. Each experiment names: the hypothesis, the success threshold, the kill threshold, the deadline. If success or kill threshold is not reached by deadline, the experiment is killed regardless. Run them.
- Write a one-page "experiment brief" template you'll use for everything: problem, hypothesis, what you're learning, kill criterion, deadline, cost cap. The brief is the gate.
- Practice killing one experiment that's "close to working." The discipline of killing the close call is harder than killing the obvious failure. Most experiments worth killing get extended because they "almost worked."

**Skill check:**
- Of three experiments started, at least two are killed on or before the deadline.
- The third survives only because it cleared the named threshold.
- No experiment got "extended just a little."
- You can name what each killed experiment taught you in one sentence.

## Stage 2: Applied Practice (weeks 4-10)

**Goal:** Run a learning-velocity-optimized portfolio. Develop experimental rigor. Build a graduation-to-production pipeline.

**Read / Watch:**
- *The Innovator's Dilemma* — Clayton Christensen. The structural reason experiments fail to graduate inside incumbents; the lessons apply to solo operators too.
- *The Innovator's Solution* — Clayton Christensen and Michael Raynor. The "what to do about it" companion.
- *Where Good Ideas Come From* — Steven Johnson. The "slow hunch" environment; experiments need a stew, not a clean lab.
- *Loonshots* — Safi Bahcall. The structure of organizations that nurture early-stage experiments. The "phase separation" metaphor matters.
- *The Idea Factory* — Jon Gertner. Bell Labs history; experimental discipline at industrial scale.
- *Skunk Works* — Ben Rich. Lockheed's experimental aerospace org; the rules of small-team rapid-prototyping.
- **Marc Andreessen's pmarchive.com essays** on product-market fit and on the "Time, Money, People" framework.
- **First Round Review** — the experiment-tagged essays are the densest free R&D-discipline writing.

**Practice:**
- Run a five-experiment portfolio over six weeks. Use 5x more experiments than usual, smaller per-experiment, faster cycles. The exercise teaches that learning-velocity scales with experiment count, not experiment size.
- Develop a "cheap teardown" discipline: every experiment must be buildable in <40 hours of work. If it takes longer, it's a project, not an experiment.
- Build a learnings ledger: every killed experiment writes a one-paragraph learning entry. Every surviving experiment writes a one-paragraph graduation entry. The ledger is the asset.
- Run a graduation review: of experiments that survived, which actually became production. Of those, which retained the original learning vs. drifted. Write the meta-learning.

**Skill check:**
- Your kill rate is high (60-80%+).
- Your time-to-kill is short.
- Your learnings ledger contains entries that have already informed downstream experiments.
- The graduated experiments are genuinely better than the un-experimented alternative.
- Your cheap-teardown discipline survived at least one tempting "let me just add one more thing" moment.

## Stage 3: Advanced Mastery (months 3-9)

**Goal:** Run an experimental portfolio at the cadence of a real R&D function. Develop novel-stack instincts. Publish experimental learnings.

**Read / Watch:**
- *The Black Swan* — Nassim Taleb. The asymmetry-of-outcomes lens; most experiments are small bets on tail outcomes.
- *Antifragile* — Nassim Taleb. The "barbell" portfolio structure; the optionality argument.
- *Where Good Ideas Come From* — Steven Johnson. Re-read for the platform-thinking chapter.
- *The Beginning of Infinity* — David Deutsch. The epistemology of explanation; experiments are explanation-tests.
- *Inventing the Future* — Nick Srnicek and Alex Williams. The systemic / political ground beneath what gets experimented on.
- *Creative Selection* — Ken Kocienda. Apple's demo-driven development discipline; how to run experiments inside a product org.
- *Hackers and Painters* — Paul Graham. The essay-anthology that frames experimental craft as a discipline.
- **The Knowledge Project podcast** — Shane Parrish; the interviews with R&D leaders and inventors compound across episodes.
- **Stripe Press books broadly** — *High Output Management*, *The Making of Prince of Persia*, *Get Together*. Each one is an experimental-craft case study.
- **Bell Labs Technical Journal** historical archive — free; if you want to see what world-class R&D documentation actually looks like, read three issues.

**Practice:**
- Run an experimental cadence at "real lab" pace: one new experiment started weekly, one killed weekly, one shipped or extended monthly. Sustain for one quarter.
- Develop a novel-stack scout: for one technology / tool / framework you don't currently use, run one experiment per month for six months. The discipline forces lateral exposure.
- Publish three experimental write-ups publicly (blog, newsletter, conference talk). Include the failures, not just the successes. The published failures are the most-read pieces.
- Run a graduation pipeline: when an experiment survives, hand it to the production function (yourself in another mode, or a colleague). Document the handoff. Track whether the graduated work retains the learning or regresses.

**Skill check:**
- Your experimental portfolio over six months produced at least one production-grade graduation.
- Multiple learnings compounded into the next cycle.
- At least one "novel stack" exposure became a real capability.
- The graduated work survives in production; the killed experiments stay killed.
- Your published failures got more reads than your published successes (this is the right ratio).

## Ongoing Development

**Stay current:**
- **First Round Review** — the experiment-tagged essays.
- **The Knowledge Project podcast** — Shane Parrish.
- **Stratechery** — Ben Thompson; the strategic context for what's worth experimenting on.
- **Hacker News** — daily; the only "feed" that surfaces novel technical experiments at scale; filter ruthlessly.
- **The Pragmatic Engineer** — Gergely Orosz; engineering-org case studies including R&D structure.
- **Paul Graham's essays** — irregular but always.
- **Sam Altman's blog** (blog.samaltman.com).

**Communities to join:**
- **YC Startup School community** — free; surface-level but high signal at the right tier.
- **Indie Hackers** — for solo-operator experiment swaps.
- **Hacker News** — read-only is fine; the threads are where most learning happens.
- **Build in Public crowd on Twitter / X** — variable but useful for cadence-of-experiment exposure.
- **Lab notebook communities (Are.na, Subconscious)** — for visual-thinker R&D peers.
- **MakerSchool, Pioneer.app community** — for early-stage solo-founder cohorts.
- A small peer R&D circle (3-5 operators) — monthly experiment-portfolio reviews.

**Quarterly cadence:**
- Pull your learnings ledger. Score the quarter: kill rate, time-to-kill, graduation rate, learnings-that-compounded count.
- Write the quarterly R&D retrospective.
- Re-audit your kill-criterion discipline: were any criteria too lenient or too strict in retrospect.
- Score the "novel stack" exposures: which ones became real capabilities; which were tourism.
- Identify the one experiment you killed too early and the one you extended too long. The pair calibrates next quarter's discipline.
