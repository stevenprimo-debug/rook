# Product Craft — Progression Path

## Who This Is For

The operator who wants to ship product that customers pay for, recommend, and keep using — not feature lists dressed up as roadmaps. Solo founders, technical co-founders, agency operators, or single-PM teams who own the spec and the shipping outcome.

By the end of this path you should be able to:
- Write a PRD that the engineering team does not bounce back.
- Run a customer-discovery cycle that produces named buyer truths.
- Cut scope on a feature you love because the math says so.
- Run a quarterly OKR review without weasel words.
- Defend a roadmap with diagnosis, policy, and action — not feature lists.
- Negotiate scope against engineering reality without overcommitting.

## Stage 1: Foundations (weeks 1-2)

**Goal:** Internalize Jobs-to-be-Done. Write your first one-page PRD. Run five real customer discovery conversations.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Competing Against Luck* — Clayton Christensen, Karen Dillon, Taddy Hall, and David Duncan. The canonical JTBD book.
- *The Mom Test* — Rob Fitzpatrick. 150-page customer-discovery primer.
- *Inspired* — Marty Cagan. The PM operating system most modern product teams quietly run on. Read chapters 1-12 first.
- *Cracking the PM Interview* — Gayle Laakmann McDowell and Jackie Bavaro. Even if not interviewing; the framework chapters teach the discipline.
- **Lenny Rachitsky's newsletter** (lennysnewsletter.com). Free posts; subscribe before paying.
- **JTBD.info** — Tony Ulwick's free articles on Outcome-Driven Innovation; the formalized side of JTBD.
- **The Re-Wired Group's JTBD courses** — Bob Moesta's lineage; the interview-craft material is the gold.
- **The Mind the Product YouTube channel** — recorded conference talks; free.

**Practice:**
- Pick a product you use daily. Write the JTBD statement for one of your uses: "When I [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]." Iterate until it feels true.
- Recruit five people who use a product in your domain. Run five Mom Test conversations (30 min each). Take notes. Do not pitch your idea.
- Write a one-page PRD for one feature: problem, jobs, scope, non-goals, success metric, rough sketch. Maximum one page. Maximum.
- Map your product's existing features against three named JTBDs. Identify the features that don't serve any job; those are candidates for retirement.
- Build a customer-interview kit: questions template, recording setup, transcription workflow, theme-extraction approach.
- Read one published case study of a great PRD (Slack's, Linear's, Stripe's). Reverse-engineer the structure.

**Skill check:**
- Hand your PRD to a builder cold. They can scope it and ask three clarifying questions. They do not ask "what does this do?" or "who is it for?" — those are answered on the page.
- You can articulate the difference between a feature request and a JTBD without thinking.
- Your five customer conversations produced at least two patterns you did not predict.

## Stage 2: Applied Practice (weeks 3-8)

**Goal:** Ship one feature end-to-end with measured outcome. Build a backlog ranking framework. Cut scope on something you love.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Continuous Discovery Habits* — Teresa Torres. The opportunity solution tree.
- *Empowered* — Marty Cagan and Chris Jones. The team-design side of *Inspired*.
- *Hooked* — Nir Eyal. Behavior design for product. The 2014 ethics chapter matters.
- *Indistractable* — Nir Eyal's follow-up that walks back parts of *Hooked* with discipline.
- *Sprint* — Jake Knapp et al. The five-day design sprint as the most concrete prototype-runbook in print.
- **Reforge** — Brian Balfour's growth and product series. Paid; one course at a time.
- **The Product Podcast** by Product School — interview format with shipping PMs.
- **Lenny's Podcast** — the deep-dive interviews are the highest-leverage 90 minutes of PM content per week.
- **Marty Cagan's "Silicon Valley Product Group" blog** (svpg.com) — free; the foundational essays.
- **Shreyas Doshi's writing on Twitter / X** — long threads on PM craft; the "what type of failure is this" framework is canon.

**Practice:**
- Ship one feature in your product (or a side project) from PRD through release. Define the success metric before you ship. Measure it 30 days after.
- Build a backlog ranking sheet using RICE (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort) or ICE. Score 10 features. Ship the top one. Skip the rest for now.
- Cut scope on a feature you love. Document what you removed and why. Re-read it in 30 days; check whether removal actually hurt outcomes.
- Run a feature post-mortem: hypothesis, prediction, outcome, gap, lesson. One page.
- Build an opportunity solution tree for one product area following Torres's method. Stress-test it with a peer.
- Develop a "customer-truth journal" — every customer call surfaces a one-line truth that goes in the journal. After 30 entries, the patterns emerge.

**Skill check:**
- Your feature shipped on time, the success metric was measured, and you can defend (or kill) the feature with data instead of vibes.
- Your backlog ranking holds up under a peer's critical read.
- You can name the three features you killed this quarter and why.

## Stage 3: Advanced Mastery (months 2-6)

**Goal:** Own a quarter of product strategy. Write a vision document. Negotiate scope against engineering reality. Run a real OKR cycle.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* — Richard Rumelt. The most useful PM strategy book.
- *Escaping the Build Trap* — Melissa Perri. The shift from output orientation to outcome orientation.
- *Measure What Matters* — John Doerr. OKR primer.
- *Radical Focus* — Christina Wodtke. The OKR-in-practice book; shorter, more useful than Doerr's.
- *The Lean Product Playbook* — Dan Olsen. The product-market-fit pyramid as the structural diagram.
- *Crossing the Chasm* — Geoffrey Moore. Required for B2B / enterprise-leaning PMs.
- *Inside the Tornado* — Moore's follow-up; the hypergrowth-stage playbook.
- *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* — Ben Horowitz. For the non-technical PM skills that show up at scale.
- *Working Backwards* — Colin Bryar and Bill Carr. Amazon's PR/FAQ method; the press-release-first discipline.
- **First Round Review** — the PM-tagged essays.
- **Stratechery** — Ben Thompson. Strategy and platform economics.
- **Shreyas Doshi's "High-Agency PM" content and his interview on Lenny's Podcast** — re-watch.
- **Camille Fournier's *The Manager's Path*** — for PM-to-management-track operators.

**Practice:**
- Write a three-page product vision document: where the product is in five years, who it serves, what it competes against, why it wins.
- Run a real OKR cycle: three objectives, three KRs each, quarterly check-ins, end-of-quarter scoring. Score honestly — most KRs miss; that's the point.
- Negotiate scope on one feature against an engineering estimate that exceeds budget. Find the cut that preserves outcome without preserving feature surface.
- Run a customer advisory board cadence: 5-7 customers, monthly call, structured questions. Six months in, the patterns show.
- Develop a quarterly strategy doc using Rumelt's diagnosis-policy-action structure. Hand it to a peer; defend each section.
- Write one PR/FAQ for a feature before you start scoping. The press-release-first discipline kills features that don't deserve to ship.

**Skill check:**
- Your quarterly strategy doc holds up under a peer's critical read. They can name the one bet, the kill criterion, and the success metric. The doc does not list 14 priorities.
- Your shipped feature this quarter moved your top OKR meaningfully.
- You can identify three things your competitors are doing better and three things you are doing better — without flinching.

- **Maggie Crowley's "Build Better Product Teams" podcast** — PM ops at scale.
- **The Knowledge Project podcast** — Shane Parrish; decision-quality interviews; useful for PM judgment.
- **Marty Cagan's "Loved" book** when it launches; check svpg.com for updates.
- **The Coda blog and the Notion blog** — case studies of product-led-growth surfaces designed in-house.
- **Reforge's annual State of Product report** — benchmarks; free if you sign up.
- **The Linear blog and Stripe Press books** — product-craft case studies in long form.

**Communities to join:**
- **Lenny's Slack community** (paid; high signal).
- **Mind the Product Slack** — free, large, regional channels useful for local meetups.
- **Reforge alumni network** — gated by course completion; high signal once in.
- **Product School Slack** — large and variable; useful for early-career PMs.
- **The PMSchool community** for the early-PM track.
- A small accountability cohort (3-5 PMs) who exchange PRDs for peer review monthly.

**Quarterly cadence:**
- Review your shipped features against your bet doc. What did you ship? What did you predict? Where did you miss? Write one page to next-quarter you.
- Re-score your OKRs honestly. Set next quarter's based on what changed.
- Run a customer-truth review: 10+ interviews, what's the pattern, what does it imply for the roadmap.
- Audit your backlog: kill anything that's been sitting for 6+ months. Stale backlog is hidden tax on the team.
