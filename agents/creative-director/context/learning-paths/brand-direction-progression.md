# Brand Direction — Progression Path

## Who This Is For

The operator who names what the work is for before any artifact gets made. Founders, agency owners, marketing leads, and solo brand-builders who need to write the brief their downstream designers, copywriters, and marketers execute against.

By the end of this path you should be able to:
- Write a positioning statement competitors cannot copy.
- Define the feeling a brand should produce in one sentence.
- Run a creative brief that survives contact with execution.
- Orchestrate writers / designers / strategists toward a coherent point of view.
- Defend a brand voice doc that a new contractor can write in on day one.
- Sustain a multi-year narrative arc across campaigns.

## Stage 1: Foundations (weeks 1-3)

**Goal:** Speak the language of positioning, voice, narrative, and visual identity. Read enough strategy and identity work that "brand" stops being a vague word.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Obviously Awesome* — April Dunford.
- *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* — Al Ries and Jack Trout.
- *Building a StoryBrand* — Donald Miller.
- *Designing Brand Identity* — Alina Wheeler.
- *Wally Olins: The Brand Handbook* — Wally Olins.
- *The Brand Gap* — Marty Neumeier.
- *Zag* — Marty Neumeier.
- *The Dictionary of Brand* — Marty Neumeier.
- **Stratechery by Ben Thompson** — the aggregation theory series.
- *Brand Identity Breakthrough* — Gregory Diehl. The independent-brand-builder lens.
- *How Brands Become Icons* — Douglas Holt. The cultural-branding theory.

**Practice:**
- Pick five brands you think are positioned well (Liquid Death, Patagonia, Stripe, Glossier, Apple in the 2000s). Write each one's positioning statement in your own words using April Dunford's structure: for [target], against [competitive alternative], we are the [category] that delivers [unique value], because [evidence].
- Write your own (or your client's) positioning statement using the same structure. Iterate ten times. The first version is wrong.
- Read three brand books / brand guidelines documents end-to-end (look for Mailchimp's, Slack's old one, Stripe's). Note what they include that yours doesn't.

**Skill check:**
- Hand your positioning statement to someone in your target audience. They can repeat it back roughly.
- The competitor named in the statement is specific enough that someone else could point to them.
- You can name the one belief the brand holds that the competitor cannot.
- Your positioning fits on one page and would survive a peer's "so what?" challenge.

## Stage 2: Applied Practice (weeks 4-10)

**Goal:** Write a brief that survives execution. Run one full campaign from positioning to creative. Develop a brand voice that holds across channels.

**Read / Watch:**
- *The 22 Immutable Laws of Branding* — Al and Laura Ries.
- *Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This* — Luke Sullivan.
- *Truth, Lies, and Advertising* — Jon Steel.
- *Eat Big Fish* — Adam Morgan. The challenger-brand playbook.
- *The Anatomy of Humbug* — Paul Feldwick. The advertising-theory critic's tour.
- **The Honest Truth podcast** — Adam Pierno.
- **Strategy Finishing School** — Mark Pollard's free content.
- **Marketing Examples** — Harry Dry. Free, weekly.
- *Brand Strategy* by Marty Neumeier (course materials and book).
- **The Brand Gap conference recordings** — Neumeier's annual sessions.

**Practice:**
- Write one creative brief for a real campaign (yours or a client's). One page. Includes: audience, position, single-minded proposition, mandatory elements, success metric. Hand it to a designer and a copywriter and watch what they produce.
- Develop a brand voice document. Include: three words the brand IS, three the brand IS NOT, sentence rhythms, vocabulary to avoid, three example paragraphs in the voice. Pressure-test by writing five new pieces of copy in the voice and having a stranger guess they were written by the same brand.
- Run one campaign from brief through execution. Brief the writer. Brief the designer. Approve or reject work against the brief, not against taste. Document where the work drifted and why.
- Audit one of your own surfaces (site, deck, social) against your positioning. Find the three places the surface drifts off-position. Fix them.

**Skill check:**
- Your designer and copywriter come back with work that you can approve or reject by pointing at the brief.
- The conversation is not about taste. The brief did the work.
- Your brand voice document survives a blind test: a stranger reads five new pieces and identifies the same brand wrote them all.
- One audit you ran on existing surface produced three concrete drift-fix tasks.

## Stage 3: Advanced Mastery (months 3-9)

**Goal:** Orchestrate a brand system across multiple disciplines. Develop a narrative arc that holds over multi-year campaigns. Hire and brief creative talent who outpaces you.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* — Richard Rumelt. The diagnosis-policy-action structure of strategy. Brand work is strategy work.
- *Playing to Win* — A.G. Lafley and Roger Martin. The where-to-play / how-to-win cascade. P&G's strategic operating system.
- *Different* — Youngme Moon. The dimensions on which most brands compete are commoditized; competitive divergence is the entire job.
- *Made to Stick* — Chip and Dan Heath. SUCCESS framework; the structural toolbox for narrative that holds.
- *The Anatomy of Story* — John Truby. Not branding; story craft. The 22 structural beats apply to brand narrative.
- *Wally Olins on Brand* — Wally Olins. Mid-career; the corporate-identity discipline.
- *Brand Thinking and Other Noble Pursuits* — Debbie Millman. Interview anthology; CDs talking shop.
- **Design Matters podcast** — Debbie Millman. 800+ episodes; the deepest free archive of creative-director conversations.
- **The Strategy Inside Everything podcast** — Adam Pierno.
- **Stratechery** — daily strategy discipline.

**Practice:**
- Develop a three-year narrative arc for one brand: where it starts, where it ends, the three campaigns in between that get it there. Document it as a one-page roadmap.
- Hire (or contract) one creative role — writer, designer, photographer — and brief them on a real piece of work. Manage the work to outcome, not to specification.
- Run a brand audit on your full surface area: site, social, email, deck, sales material, packaging. Score each surface against the position. Identify the weakest surface; rebuild it.
- Write a brand voice training doc that lets a new contractor write in the voice on day one. The voice doc is the test of whether you have actually nailed the voice.

**Skill check:**
- A new contractor reads your brand brief and voice doc, ships work, and the work feels like your brand.
- The brief did the work. You did not have to redirect mid-execution.
- Your three-year narrative arc is on track at year two: the brand is still pointing where you said.
- A peer creative director reads your brand-system audit and signs off on the diagnosis.

- **It's Nice That** — daily; UK design + brand culture.
- **The Working Not Working newsletter** — global creative industry pulse.
- **The Pitch Lab podcast** — interview format with brand strategists.

**Communities to join:**
- **AIGA local chapter** — for in-person creative-director peer access.
- **Strategy Finishing School** — Mark Pollard's community.
- A small peer-CD circle (3-5 directors) — monthly brief swaps; structured critique.
- **Substack creator-economy circles** — for solo-brand-builder peer access.
- **Brand Strategy Network on LinkedIn** — variable but useful for craft questions.
- **The One Show, Cannes Lions, D&AD** — annual creative-industry awards; the case-study archives are the keepers.
- **Foster (foster.co)** — premium writing-and-brand community.
- **Local AAF (American Advertising Federation) chapter** — in-person matters.

**Quarterly cadence:**
- Pull every brief you wrote last quarter. Pull the work that came out of each one. Score the gap. Rewrite the worst brief in your file from scratch using everything you now know.
- Re-audit one of the brand's surfaces against the position. Find the three drift points; fix them or brief them for fix.
- Re-read one foundational text (Dunford, Ries, Neumeier, or Olins); identify what you can use now that you couldn't six months ago.
- Score the brand voice doc against five new pieces of shipped work. If the voice held, the doc works. If it didn't, the doc needs an update.
