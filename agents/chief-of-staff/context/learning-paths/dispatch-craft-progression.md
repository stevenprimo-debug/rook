# Dispatch Craft — Progression Path

## Who This Is For

The operator who runs a one-person org with many agents (human or AI), and needs to triage every inbound spitball, hunch, opportunity, or "what should I do with this" into a clean DEPLOY / ASSIGN / PARK decision without losing anything to a backlog hole. Founders, solo executives, agency principals, and anyone running an agent-augmented operating system.

By the end of this path you should be able to:
- Classify a request in one sentence.
- Route to DEPLOY / ASSIGN / PARK without ambiguity.
- Gate every irreversible action with explicit confirm.
- Orchestrate parallel workstreams without dropping a thread.
- Run a weekly review that produces a clean "what's next" inside 30 minutes.
- Train another operator on your operating system end-to-end.

## Stage 1: Foundations (weeks 1-3)

**Goal:** Internalize delegation, reversibility-thinking, and the discipline of writing a brief that survives execution without you.

**Read / Watch:**
- *The Effective Executive* — Peter Drucker.
- *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* — Ben Horowitz.
- *Who Not How* — Dan Sullivan and Benjamin Hardy.
- *The Score Takes Care of Itself* — Bill Walsh.
- *High Output Management* — Andy Grove.
- *Only the Paranoid Survive* — Andy Grove. The follow-up; the strategic-inflection-point chapter is canon.
- *Output: An Operations Manual for Founders Stuck in the Middle* — Anh Ho.
- *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* — Ben Horowitz. The chief-of-staff function is a hard-things function.
- **Cedric Chin's "Commoncog" blog** (commoncog.com).
- **The Founder's Operations Handbook** by Akshay Sharma.
- **The Pragmatic Engineer** — Gergely Orosz; for the engineering-management adjacency.

**Practice:**
- Write 20 one-sentence classifications of incoming requests from your last two weeks of work. Each classification names the type of work and proposes a route (do now / brief out / park).
- Build your DEPLOY / ASSIGN / PARK template — what each route looks like in your context. Write three example briefs (one per route).
- Identify the five irreversible actions in your week (sends, posts, money moves, public commitments, hires/fires). Build a gate for each: confirm-before-act. Test the gate at least once.

**Skill check:**
- Hand a peer a stream of 10 inbound requests from your inbox. They can predict your classification and route call accurately 8 out of 10 times.
- Your operating system has become legible to others.
- You can name the five irreversible actions from last week and the gate that fired on each.
- Your DEPLOY / ASSIGN / PARK template is documented and reusable by a stand-in operator.

## Stage 2: Applied Practice (weeks 4-10)

**Goal:** Orchestrate parallel workstreams. Run a structured weekly review. Develop reversibility instincts.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Getting Things Done* — David Allen. The system most operators bend; the inbox-to-zero discipline is the spine.
- *Deep Work* — Cal Newport. The protected-time discipline that makes dispatch possible at all.
- *Indistractable* — Nir Eyal. The defensive discipline against your own attention drift.
- *The 4-Hour Workweek* — Tim Ferriss. Read for the elimination-then-automation cascade; ignore the lifestyle marketing.
- *Buy Back Your Time* — Dan Martell. The dollar-value-per-hour discipline that drives delegation choices.
- *The E-Myth Revisited* — Michael Gerber. The systems-vs-technician distinction; the chief-of-staff function is the systems function.
- **Lenny Rachitsky's newsletter** — operator interviews; useful for seeing how senior leaders structure their week.
- **First Round Review** — operator and executive essays.

**Practice:**
- Run a weekly review for four weeks straight. Same template each week: what landed, what got parked, what got assigned, what's next. Track classification accuracy week-over-week.
- Build a parked-items resurfacing system: every park has an idea-specific trigger that resurfaces it. Test the trigger at least once per parked item.
- Run one complex orchestration: a project that requires three different specialists (or three different sub-agents) executing in parallel. Write the briefs. Track the handoffs. Document what dropped.
- Develop a reversibility taxonomy: which actions are one-way doors, which are two-way, which look like one-way but are actually two-way. The taxonomy is the gate's source of truth.

**Skill check:**
- Your weekly review produces a clean "what's next" within 30 minutes.
- Nothing parked last quarter is rotting in a backlog hole.
- You can name the three irreversible actions you took last week and defend each one.
- Your reversibility taxonomy has fired on at least one would-be mistake.
- Your parked-items resurfacing system has at least 5 successful re-surfaces (the trigger fired; the item got handled).

## Stage 3: Advanced Mastery (months 3-9)

**Goal:** Run an agent-augmented operating system. Develop scope-expansion judgment. Orchestrate at portfolio scale.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* — Richard Rumelt. The diagnosis-policy-action structure that survives the noise.
- *Thinking in Bets* — Annie Duke. Decision-quality versus outcome-quality; the dispatch function lives in decision-quality.
- *How to Decide* — Annie Duke. The follow-up; more operational.
- *The Manager's Path* — Camille Fournier. Engineering management ladder; the dispatcher's playbook for technical work.
- *Staff Engineer* — Will Larson. The non-management technical-leadership track; chief-of-staff borrows heavily from this lineage.
- *Output: An Operations Manual for Founders Stuck in the Middle* — Anh Ho. Published essays on operator-as-orchestrator.
- *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team* — Patrick Lencioni. The trust foundation that delegation rests on.
- **Cedric Chin's "Commoncog" Operator** content (paid) — high-signal operator case studies.
- **Stratechery** — Ben Thompson; the strategic-context discipline.
- **The Knowledge Project podcast** — Shane Parrish; high-signal interviews with operators and decision scientists.

**Practice:**
- Design and operate a one-person multi-agent system: which agents handle which classes of work, how do they hand off, where does the orchestration log live. Document the system. Run it for 30 days. Audit it.
- Develop a scope-expansion playbook: when to push back on a request for being too small, when to challenge a request for being too narrow. The default is execution; the senior move is to ask whether the right thing is being asked.
- Run a quarterly portfolio review: what did you assign, what did you park, what got resurfaced, what compounded. Score yourself against the bets you made at the start of the quarter.
- Train a peer on your operating system end-to-end. The training is the test of whether the system is legible.

**Skill check:**
- A peer who shadows your operating week can predict your DEPLOY / ASSIGN / PARK calls correctly 9 out of 10 times.
- Your reversibility-gate has fired at least once on something it should have fired on.
- You've trained at least one peer or stand-in on your operating system end-to-end.
- Your quarterly portfolio review surfaces compounding patterns the previous quarter's review did not.
