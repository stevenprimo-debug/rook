# Marketing Orchestration — Progression Path

## Who This Is For

The operator who owns marketing strategy — positioning, audience, campaign architecture, channel mix, the brief that downstream content / design / social / paid execute against — and refuses to confuse "we ran a campaign" with "we built an audience the brand owns." Founders, CMOs, marketing leads, agency principals, and solo-operators where the brand is the moat.

By the end of this path you should be able to:
- Position against a real competitor with a one-page statement.
- Write a campaign brief that downstream executors don't bounce.
- Build an owned-audience asset that compounds.
- Hold brand coherence across surfaces and cycles.
- Develop a 12-month narrative arc across multiple campaigns.
- Orchestrate cross-functional execution (content / design / social / paid / sales).

## Stage 1: Foundations (weeks 1-3)

**Goal:** Internalize positioning. Define audience. Build your first campaign brief.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Obviously Awesome* — April Dunford. The most useful positioning book published in the last decade. Read twice; the chapter on "five questions before positioning" is mandatory.
- *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* — Al Ries and Jack Trout. The 1981 classic; everything since is a footnote.
- *Crossing the Chasm* — Geoffrey Moore. The B2B technology-adoption frame; the early-majority targeting discipline is the keeper.
- *Influence* — Robert Cialdini. The persuasion-craft underneath all marketing.
- *Made to Stick* — Chip and Dan Heath. The SUCCESS framework; the structural toolbox for memorable campaigns.
- **April Dunford's content on YouTube and at firstround.com** — the talks compound across viewings.
- **Stratechery** — Ben Thompson; the strategic-context discipline that positioning lives inside.
- **First Round Review** — the marketing-tagged essays.
- The vendored reference clippings `context/references/hubspot-academy.md` and `hubspot-academy-1.md` for inbound and marketing-operations fundamentals.

**Practice:**
- Write your (or your client's) positioning statement using April Dunford's structure: for [target], against [competitive alternative], we are the [category] that delivers [unique value], because [evidence]. Iterate 10 times. The first version is wrong.
- Define your audience in writing: persona, demographics, psychographics, jobs-to-be-done, the alternative they currently use, what they say when they describe the problem. The persona is the spine of every campaign.
- Build one campaign brief: objective, audience, position, single-minded proposition, mandatory elements, success metric, timeline. One page. Hand it to a writer and a designer; watch what they produce.

**Skill check:**
- Your positioning fits on one page.
- Your audience description is specific enough that a peer can describe back the persona unprompted.
- Your campaign brief produced work that hit the brief on the first or second draft, not the fifth.
- You can name the competitor your positioning differentiates against specifically.

## Stage 2: Applied Practice (weeks 4-10)

**Goal:** Build a channel-mix strategy. Run one full campaign. Develop owned-audience discipline.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Different* — Youngme Moon. The competitive-divergence frame; the dimensions on which most brands compete are commoditized, and divergence is the entire game.
- *Eat Big Fish* — Adam Morgan. The challenger-brand playbook; the eight credos hold.
- *Hacking Growth* — Sean Ellis and Morgan Brown. The growth-team operating manual; useful even if you don't have a team.
- *Traction* — Gabriel Weinberg and Justin Mares. The 19 channels framework; the discipline of "test channels before scaling channels" is the keeper.
- *Contagious* — Jonah Berger. The STEPPS framework for what spreads.
- **Reforge's growth-and-marketing courses** (reforge.com) — paid; one course at a time; the writing on the public blog is free.
- **Animalz blog** — content-marketing-leaning but the strategic chapters apply broadly.
- **Stratechery** — daily.
- **The CMO Confidential podcast** — Mike Linton; senior-marketer interviews.
- **Marketing Brew** — daily marketing news.

**Practice:**
- Build a channel-mix strategy: list every available channel (SEO, paid search, paid social, organic social, email, content, podcast, PR, partnerships, events). Score each against three criteria: audience-fit (does our audience live there?), CAC viability (does the unit economics work?), strategic-fit (does it build owned audience or rent it?). Pick three to invest in; defer the rest.
- Run one full campaign end-to-end: brief → executors → ship → measure → post-mortem. Pick a measurable objective (signups, demos, MQLs, revenue, list growth). Hit or miss it; both teach.
- Develop owned-audience discipline: pick the owned-audience asset (email list, community, podcast, customer base). Every campaign drives toward it. Every campaign is measured by its contribution to it. The discipline is what separates marketing from advertising.
- Build a brand-voice document and stress-test it: write five pieces of marketing copy across three formats (email, landing page, social post). Have a stranger read all five and identify whether the same brand wrote them. The voice document is the test.

**Skill check:**
- Your three-channel strategy is producing measurable audience growth.
- Your one campaign hit or missed its objective with a clear reason why.
- Your owned-audience asset is growing.
- Your brand voice document survives a blind test against three competitors.

## Stage 3: Advanced Mastery (months 3-12)

**Goal:** Build narrative arc across multi-quarter campaigns. Develop B2B-and-B2C marketing fluency. Orchestrate marketing as a system.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* — Richard Rumelt. The diagnosis-policy-action structure that makes strategy real instead of fluff.
- *Playing to Win* — A.G. Lafley and Roger Martin. The where-to-play / how-to-win cascade; P&G's strategic operating system.
- *Hidden in Plain Sight* — Erich Joachimsthaler. The "hidden assets" frame for repositioning a category.
- *Lovemarks* — Kevin Roberts. The Saatchi & Saatchi CEO's brand-as-emotional-asset frame; uneven but the case studies hold.
- *The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing* — Al and Laura Ries. The companion to *Positioning*; the law of category and the law of focus are canon.
- *Marketing Warfare* — Al Ries and Jack Trout. The military-metaphor frame for competitive marketing; dated language, intact structure.
- *Crossing the Chasm*, *Inside the Tornado*, *Living on the Fault Line* — the Geoffrey Moore trilogy; mandatory for B2B technology marketing.
- *How Brands Grow* and *How Brands Grow Part 2* — Byron Sharp. The Ehrenberg-Bass Institute research on what actually drives growth; counterintuitive and often-cited.
- *The Long and the Short of It* — Les Binet and Peter Field. The IPA research on brand-building vs. activation; the 60/40 split argument.
- *Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This* — Luke Sullivan. The agency-creative-director craft book.
- *Truth, Lies, and Advertising* — Jon Steel. The account-planner discipline.
- **The CMO Confidential podcast** — Mike Linton.
- **Marketing Over Coffee** — John Wall and Christopher Penn; weekly.
- **The Bedrock Effect podcast** — Robert Rose.
- **The Drum, AdAge, AdWeek** — for industry-pulse; pick one.
- **Marketoonist (Tom Fishburne)** — weekly cartoon-driven marketing essay; useful counterweight to taking yourself too seriously.

**Practice:**
- Develop a 12-month narrative arc for the brand: where it starts, where it ends, the three campaigns in between that get it there. Document as a one-page roadmap.
- Run a marketing-operations review: martech stack audit (CRM, MAP, analytics, attribution, content management), data flow, attribution model, reporting cadence. Identify the one move that compounds; ship it.
- Orchestrate one cross-functional campaign: brief downstream writers, designers, paid-media operators, sales (for handoff). Manage to outcome, not to specification. Document where work drifted and why.
- Build a personal benchmark-against-research discipline: every quarter, read one Ehrenberg-Bass or IPA paper, one strategy / positioning paper, one channel-specific paper (Reforge, Animalz, etc.). The discipline is the only protection against vibes-based marketing.

**Skill check:**
- Your 12-month narrative arc is on track at quarter 3.
- Your cross-functional campaign produced work that all executors are proud of and that hit the brief.
- Your owned-audience asset is the single largest source of qualified demand.
- You can defend your channel-mix with unit economics, not vibes.
- Your martech stack is documented and would survive your departure.

- Pull every campaign you shipped last quarter. Score against objectives. Identify the patterns of hits and misses. Update the channel-mix strategy and the brand voice with the lesson. Write the quarterly marketing retrospective.
