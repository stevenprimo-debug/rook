# Hook, Cadence, Platform-Native

## What This Framework Is

Hook, Cadence, Platform-Native is the three-part discipline that
governs every social media post and channel strategy. The
framework holds that **social media is a scroll-stopping
medium**, not a publishing medium — and that posts which ignore
this fail predictably regardless of how good the underlying
content is.

The three parts:

1. **Hook** — the first 1-2 seconds of the post that determines
   whether the reader stops scrolling. The hook is the entire
   post for most readers; only readers the hook converts engage
   with what follows.

2. **Cadence** — the rhythm at which a channel publishes, which
   establishes audience expectation and signals to algorithm. Too
   slow = audience forgets; too fast = audience tunes out; just
   right = compounding presence.

3. **Platform-Native** — the discipline of producing content
   that fits the platform's native conventions, not generic
   "social media content" cross-posted across platforms. LinkedIn
   native is different from Twitter native is different from
   Instagram native is different from TikTok native. Cross-posted
   content under-performs on every platform.

The framework's spine: social media is not "publishing things
people might enjoy." Social media is competing for 1-3 seconds
of scroll attention, against billions of competing posts, with
content shaped for the specific platform's conventions.

## Why It Matters For This Agent

Social Media Manager's bench gates on three principles: Hook-First-Pole,
Cadence-Discipline-Pole, and Platform-Native-Pole. The framework is
the operating implementation of all three.

- **Hook-First-Pole** asks: "Does this post earn the scroll-stop
  in the first 1-2 seconds?" The framework's hook standards are
  the gate.

- **Cadence-Discipline-Pole** asks: "Is this channel publishing
  at a cadence that builds presence?" The framework's cadence
  standards are the gate.

- **Platform-Native-Pole** asks: "Does this post fit the
  platform's native conventions, or is it cross-posted generic
  content?" The framework's platform-native standards are the
  gate.

For the operator's social presence:
- **LinkedIn** (professional audience, B2B for [your employer] + [your product]
  long-form positioning).
- **Twitter/X** (practitioner audience for the cohort,
  [your physical/SaaS product] updates, AV-industry thinking).
- **YouTube/short-form** (Stack cohort marketing, tool
  demonstrations).
- **Discord** (community-anchored, not algorithmic — different
  discipline).

Each platform requires the framework applied to its specific
conventions.

## Core Concepts

### 1. The Hook Standard

The hook is the first 1-2 seconds of the post. On text posts,
this is the first line. On video, it's the first 1-2 seconds.
On image posts, it's what the image and the first line do
together.

Strong hooks do three things in 1-2 seconds:
- **Pattern interrupt** — break the scrolling rhythm.
- **Specific** — name a specific reader, scenario, or stake.
- **Promise** — signal that the rest of the post will reward the
  stop.

Hook patterns that work:
- **Contrarian take**: "Most touring engineers use QLab. Here's
  why that's about to change."
- **Specific stake**: "The session musicians you played with
  last year are using Suno. The gigs you got from them in 2023
  are gone in 2026."
- **Surprising fact**: "47% of [your customer projects] in 2025 still ran
  cues from a single laptop with no backup. The 2026 incidents
  rate will be much higher."
- **Direct address**: "If you're a [your customer industry] pro reading this,
  you have about 18 months."
- **Tension between two truths**: "AI tools are everywhere. The
  practitioners who matter are still using almost none of them.
  Here's why both are true."

Hook patterns that fail:
- Generic openers: "Excited to share...", "Wanted to talk about...",
  "Have you ever wondered...".
- Long warmup: paragraphs before the actual point.
- Buried specifics: the hook exists in paragraph 3.
- AI-default warmth: "Hope you're having a great Wednesday!".

### 2. The Cadence Standard

Cadence is platform-specific. Each platform has a sweet spot
where the algorithm rewards consistent presence without burning
out the audience.

LinkedIn:
- **Sweet spot**: 3-5 posts/week.
- **Mix**: 1 long-form thought-piece, 2-3 short text or
  image posts, 0-1 video.
- **Time-of-day**: weekday mornings (Tue-Thu) US business hours.

Twitter/X:
- **Sweet spot**: 1-3 posts/day, multiple threads/week.
- **Mix**: thought pieces, replies, threads, occasional reshares.
- **Time-of-day**: morning + late afternoon weekdays.

YouTube Shorts / TikTok:
- **Sweet spot**: 3-5 posts/week minimum; daily during launch
  pushes.
- **Mix**: hook-driven short clips, demos, micro-stories.
- **Time-of-day**: evening for cohort-target audience.

Long-form YouTube:
- **Sweet spot**: 1-2 videos/week if growing channel; less if
  the channel is mature with established audience.
- **Mix**: deep-dive content, case studies, tutorials.

Cadence-discipline isn't volume-for-volume's-sake. It's
sustainable consistency that signals to algorithms (and
audience) that the channel is alive.

### 3. The Platform-Native Standard

Each platform has native conventions. Producing content for the
platform means following these conventions, not cross-posting
generic content.

LinkedIn native:
- Long-form text (not micro-text from Twitter).
- Personal narrative + business insight.
- Hooks that work on text (no auto-play visual).
- Document carousels for educational content.
- Comments-as-content (substantive responses extend reach).
- No hashtag stuffing.

Twitter/X native:
- Concise hooks (the medium punishes verbosity).
- Threads for longer thoughts (each tweet earns the next).
- Reply chains build community.
- Quote-tweets engage with industry discussion.
- Visual content drives algorithmic priority.

YouTube Shorts / TikTok native:
- Hook in first 1.5 seconds (literal, visual).
- Pattern-interrupt visuals or audio.
- Captions for sound-off viewing.
- Cliffhangers between segments to hold scroll.
- Trends and audio (when authentic to brand) extend reach.

Instagram native (Reels + carousels):
- Visual-first hooks.
- Carousel-as-storytelling for educational content.
- Audio-trend alignment for Reels.
- Captions can be longer; first line is the hook.

The discipline: every post is produced for its platform. Cross-posts
get adapted, not duplicated.

### 4. The Voice Modulation by Platform

Brand voice is consistent across platforms; tonal register flexes
to platform native conventions.

For [your product]:
- **LinkedIn**: practitioner-to-practitioner, longer, more
  business-context.
- **Twitter/X**: practitioner-to-practitioner, more direct, faster
  rhythm.
- **YouTube/Shorts**: practitioner-to-practitioner, more visual,
  hook-driven.

The underlying voice is the same: specific, anti-warmth-default,
practitioner-centric. The register flexes.

### 5. The Engagement Loop

Posts don't end when published. The engagement loop is part of the
post's performance:

- **First-hour activity** — author and team engage with early
  replies; signals algorithm the post is alive.
- **Reply quality** — substantive replies (not just thanks) extend
  reach and build community.
- **Cross-engagement** — author engaging with related industry
  conversations during the same period builds presence beyond
  the single post.

The discipline: schedule first-hour engagement time when posting.
Posts published-and-abandoned underperform posts that get the
engagement loop run.

### 6. The Content Mix

A healthy channel mixes content types. Pure self-promotion fails;
pure educational content under-converts. Standard mix:

- **40% educational** — practitioner insights, industry analysis,
  how-to content. Builds authority.
- **30% perspective** — opinion pieces, contrarian takes,
  industry commentary. Builds voice.
- **20% community** — engaging with industry conversations,
  amplifying practitioners' work, replying substantively.
  Builds network.
- **10% direct promotion** — cohort announcements, product
  launches, specific CTAs. Builds business outcomes.

The 10% direct promotion converts because the 90% non-promotion
built trust and presence. Channels that flip this ratio (80%
promotion) burn out fast.

### 7. The Brand-Story Through-Line

Across all posts and platforms, a coherent brand story builds.
Individual posts contribute to the story; the story is what
audiences remember.

For [your product]:
- **The story arc**: practitioner watching industry shift, building
  tools and community for the practitioners coming next.
- **Recurring themes**: tools that respect practitioners,
  community that compounds, specific gigs and outcomes, AI
  tooling that earns its place not just looks impressive.
- **Anti-themes**: hype-driven AI narratives, generic productivity
  content, "AI will replace you" fear-marketing.

Every post sits inside this story. Posts that drift from the
through-line dilute the brand; posts that reinforce it compound.

## Common Applications

**LinkedIn weekly cadence:**
The agent plans 4 LinkedIn posts for the week. Monday: industry-
analysis thought piece (hook: contrarian take on AI in [your customer industry]).
Tuesday: short post amplifying a practitioner's work in the
[your community] (community). Thursday: educational carousel on
cohort program structure. Friday: perspective piece on the
creator-economy shift. Cadence held; mix balanced; each platform-
native.

**Cohort-launch direct-promotion week:**
During launch week, direct-promotion ratio rises to 30% briefly.
The other 70% remains educational/perspective/community to
prevent burn-out signal. Specific posts: launch announcement,
applicant testimonials, FAQ-style content, single-day deadline
push.

**Cross-platform adaptation, not duplication:**
A new [your physical/SaaS product] feature drops. LinkedIn version: long-form
explanation with business context (cost savings, reliability,
case-study scenarios). Twitter version: feature thread (hook
tweet, then 5-7 tweet thread). YouTube Shorts version: hook
visual + demonstration in 45 seconds. Same feature; three
platform-native treatments.

**Hook iteration on underperforming post:**
A post underperforms. The agent diagnoses: was the hook weak?
Pattern-interrupt missing? Specificity buried? Often, rewriting
the first line and re-publishing later produces dramatically
different performance. Hook is the highest-leverage edit.

**Engagement-loop scheduling:**
When scheduling a post, the agent blocks 30-60 minutes of author
time post-publication for engagement-loop. Replies to comments,
engages with adjacent industry conversations, reshares related
content. The loop is part of the post's performance plan.

**Per locked feedback: "No constraint-aware Mention in Public Marketing."**
The agent enforces this on all social posts. constraint-aware context is
internal; public framing uses "I built the team I needed"
language.

**Per locked feedback: "Client Data Publication Rule."** Social
posts do not include sensitive client data — even case studies
require strict adherence to publication rule.

## Anti-patterns (when this framework is misapplied)

**Cross-posting generic content.** Same post on LinkedIn,
Twitter, and Instagram. Each platform's algorithm and audience
under-rewards content that doesn't fit native conventions. Every
post should be adapted to its platform.

**Hook-free posting.** Opening with warm-up, throat-clearing, or
generic context-setting. The scroll-stop fails; the rest of the
post doesn't matter because nobody reads it.

**Volume over signal.** Posting daily because "consistency
matters" without distinctive content. Audience tunes out the
channel that publishes constantly without distinctive value.

**Pure self-promotion.** Channels that post 80% promotional
content burn out the audience and the algorithm. The content mix
discipline is the prevention.

**Per locked feedback: "Execute, Don't Preamble."** Social posts
operationalize this. The first line is the hook — not warmup.

**Per locked feedback: "Avoid Text Wrapping at All Costs."**
Headlines, captions, alt-text that wrap on standard previews
fail. Shorten.

**Per locked feedback: "No emojis in client-facing email" extends
to [your employer]-related LinkedIn posts.** Executive-buyer register on
LinkedIn refuses emoji clutter on serious B2B posts. Casual posts
on personal the operator accounts have different register.

**Per locked feedback: "Brand to the customer's trade."** Social
content for the AV-practitioner audience uses trade-specific
language and references; generic startup framing fails this
audience.

**Per locked feedback: "Match Execution Mode."** Social cadence
respects this — sometimes the choice is 80%-version-now vs.
100%-version-late. Cadence preservation may justify the 80%
ship.

**Engagement-loop abandonment.** Posting and walking away. The
algorithm and the audience reward first-hour engagement; abandoning
the post degrades performance.

**Cadence drift.** Posting in bursts (5 in a week, then nothing
for 3 weeks). Algorithm-presence decays during silent periods.
Sustainable cadence is the discipline.
