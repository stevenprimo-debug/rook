# Accounting Framework

## What This Framework Is

The accounting framework is the structured language businesses use to
record economic events and produce three core artifacts: the income
statement, the balance sheet, and the statement of cash flows. Together,
these three statements answer different questions:

- **Income statement** — Over a period, did the business produce profit?
  (Revenue minus expenses.)
- **Balance sheet** — At a moment in time, what does the business own
  and owe? (Assets = Liabilities + Equity.)
- **Cash flow statement** — Over a period, where did cash come from and
  where did it go? (Operating + Investing + Financing.)

The framework is governed by GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting
Principles) in the US and IFRS internationally. Both share core
principles: accrual basis (revenue recorded when earned, not when cash
hits), the matching principle (expenses recorded in the period they
help produce revenue), and the going-concern assumption (the business
will continue operating in the foreseeable future).

For a solo operator running multiple revenue streams ([your employer] W-2,
the Stack product income, side-engagement consulting), the framework
applies in compressed form. The three statements collapse into one
question: **is the engine producing cash that compounds the freedom
fund, or is it producing activity that consumes attention without
producing wealth?**

## Why It Matters For This Agent

Finance Manager's bench gates on three principles: Numbers-Honest-Pole,
Compounding-First-Pole, and Mission-Alignment-Pole. The accounting
framework supplies the vocabulary for the first pole — what does
"numbers honest" mean in practice?

- Honest numbers separate **revenue** from **collections** (a $50K [your employer]
  deal closed in May does not become cash until invoicing-billing-payment
  completes, often 30-90 days later).
- Honest numbers separate **profit** from **cash flow** (a profitable
  month can still be cash-negative if customers pay slowly and the
  business pays vendors quickly).
- Honest numbers separate **gross margin** from **net income** (a $1M
  [your employer] deal at 20% GP is $200K gross profit, but after the operator's split,
  taxes, and overhead, net income is a much smaller number).

The Compounding-First-Pole uses accounting data to answer: "Is this
month's economic activity moving the freedom fund forward?" The
Mission-Alignment-Pole uses it to answer: "Is the activity producing
wealth-creation income (the Stack SaaS, Ableton products) or just
trading time for cash ([your employer] W-2, consulting)?"

## Core Concepts

### 1. The Accounting Equation

**Assets = Liabilities + Equity.** Every economic event affects at
least two accounts in a way that preserves this equation. Cash spent
on a laptop: assets (cash) down, assets (equipment) up — equation
holds. Loan taken to fund inventory: assets (cash) up, liabilities
(loan) up — equation holds.

For the operator: the equation is the structural check that nothing
is being recorded in a vacuum. Every dollar in has a source; every
dollar out has a destination.

### 2. Accrual vs. Cash Basis

**Cash basis** records revenue when cash arrives and expenses when
cash leaves. Simple but distorted — a $100K deal closed in December
that pays in February shows zero December revenue.

**Accrual basis** records revenue when earned (deal closed, product
delivered, service performed) regardless of when cash arrives, and
records expenses when incurred regardless of when paid. This produces
a truer picture of economic activity in the period but requires
tracking accounts receivable (revenue earned, not yet collected) and
accounts payable (expenses incurred, not yet paid).

For the operator: track both. Accrual answers "what did I produce
this period?" Cash answers "what did I actually have to spend?" Both
are required for honest decisions.

### 3. The Income Statement Structure

```
Revenue
  - Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
= Gross Profit
  - Operating Expenses
= Operating Income (EBIT)
  - Interest Expense
  - Taxes
= Net Income
```

Key ratios derived from this structure:

- **Gross margin** = Gross Profit / Revenue. For [your employer] deals, the
  threshold is 15% minimum, 30%+ ideal. Gross margin reveals whether
  the core product economics work.
- **Operating margin** = Operating Income / Revenue. Reveals whether
  the business operates efficiently after gross margin is captured.
- **Net margin** = Net Income / Revenue. The final, after-everything
  number. For solo operators, this is the number that funds the
  freedom fund.

### 4. The Balance Sheet Structure

```
ASSETS                       LIABILITIES + EQUITY
Current Assets                Current Liabilities
  Cash                          Accounts Payable
  Accounts Receivable           Short-term Debt
  Inventory                     Accrued Expenses
Long-term Assets              Long-term Liabilities
  Property/Equipment            Long-term Debt
  Intangible Assets             Deferred Revenue
                              Equity
                                Retained Earnings
                                Owner Capital
```

Key insights from the balance sheet:

- **Working capital** = Current Assets − Current Liabilities. Reveals
  short-term liquidity. Negative working capital means short-term
  obligations exceed short-term resources — a survival flag.
- **Current ratio** = Current Assets / Current Liabilities. Healthy
  is 1.5-3.0. Below 1.0 is distress; above 3.0 may indicate cash
  sitting idle that should be deployed.
- **Debt-to-equity** = Total Liabilities / Equity. Reveals leverage.
  High leverage amplifies returns and losses; low leverage produces
  resilience.

### 5. The Cash Flow Statement Structure

```
Cash from Operating Activities
  Net Income
  + Non-cash expenses (depreciation, amortization)
  +/- Changes in working capital
= Operating Cash Flow

Cash from Investing Activities
  - Capital expenditures
  +/- Asset sales/purchases

Cash from Financing Activities
  + New debt / equity issued
  - Debt repayment / dividends paid

= Net Change in Cash
```

The cash flow statement reconciles accrual-basis net income with the
actual cash that flowed through the business. A profitable business
can fail if operating cash flow is structurally negative — this is the
single most common cause of small-business failure.

### 6. Revenue Recognition Principle

Revenue is recognized when:
1. Performance obligation is satisfied (product delivered, service
   performed).
2. Collection is reasonably assured.
3. Amount is determinable.

For [your employer]: revenue recognized at install completion, not at deal
close. For SaaS subscriptions: revenue recognized ratably over the
subscription period (a $1200 annual subscription = $100/month
recognized, not $1200 upfront). For Ableton product sales: revenue
recognized at point of sale.

This matters because: a strong "deal close" month can be a weak
"revenue recognized" month if installs lag.

### 7. The Matching Principle

Expenses are recorded in the period they help produce revenue. A $5K
ad spend in May that produces $20K in July sales is matched: the
expense recognition aligns with the revenue it generated. This is
why accrual accounting requires accounts payable tracking — the
expense is recorded when incurred (May), not when paid (whenever
the credit card statement is due).

For the operator: this principle is the basis for understanding ROI
on marketing spend, on tool subscriptions, on time invested in a
product line. If the time invested in the Stack in Q2 produces SaaS
revenue in Q4, the matching principle is what allows that ROI to be
seen clearly rather than smeared across periods.

## Common Applications

**Monthly cash-flow audit:**
The agent pulls operating cash flow, separates wealth-creation income
(the Stack SaaS, Ableton products) from time-trading income ([your employer] W-2,
consulting), and reports the ratio. If wealth-creation income is
flat across three months while time-trading income absorbs the
operator's attention, Mission-Alignment-Pole flags the drift.

**[your employer] deal gross-margin gate:**
A $400K [your employer] opportunity at 12% GP arrives. The agent applies the
locked auto-reject rule: <15% GP triggers rejection. The opportunity
is declined before time is invested. Numbers-Honest-Pole gated on
GP, not on revenue glamour.

**Tax-quarter readiness:**
Q3 close. The agent estimates Q3 tax liability from year-to-date
income, compares against estimated payments made, and flags the
shortfall (or surplus) for Q4 payment planning. Accrual basis income
times effective tax rate, less estimated payments, equals owe.

**Freedom-fund compounding check:**
The agent reads year-to-date net income, subtracts personal expenses,
subtracts taxes, identifies the surplus, and asks: was the surplus
deposited into the freedom fund or absorbed back into lifestyle?
The freedom fund must compound monthly or the exit-by-Dec-2026 math
fails.

**Working-capital warning:**
Three large [your employer] deals close in the same month, but invoicing and
payment cycle is 45-60 days. The agent identifies accounts receivable
growth without corresponding cash growth and flags the working-capital
gap: the operator may need a line of credit or earlier-invoicing
discipline to bridge the cash gap.

## Anti-patterns (when this framework is misapplied)

**Revenue glamour.** Reporting "I closed $500K in May!" without
separating revenue from collections, gross profit from revenue, and
net income from gross profit. The honest answer to "did I produce
wealth in May?" is downstream of all three filters. Per locked
feedback: "Sales targets are honest about commission, not revenue."

**Cash basis as the only view.** Running the business on cash basis
alone produces decisions based on payment timing rather than economic
activity. A month with no collections but $200K of deals closed is
not a zero-production month; it is a month with significant
accounts-receivable growth.

**Treating gross margin as net.** A $1M deal at 25% GP is $250K
gross profit. After the operator's commission split, taxes, and overhead,
the net to the freedom fund may be $40-60K. Reporting "$250K profit"
on this deal is dishonest math that overstates progress toward
exit.

**Ignoring the matching principle.** A heavy month of the Stack
content production may show as pure expense — but the matching
principle says: this expense will be matched against future SaaS
revenue. Without the matching frame, the operator over-rotates on
"cut the Stack spend" decisions during low-revenue months.

**Confusing wealth-creation income with time-trading income.** [your employer]
W-2 and [your employer] commission are both income, but they are not wealth
creation. They are time traded for cash. the Stack SaaS and Ableton
product royalties are wealth creation — the income continues without
ongoing time input. Mission-Alignment-Pole gates on this distinction.

**Off-balance-sheet activity.** Cash spent on a personal credit card
for business expenses, deals worked without a paper trail, side
engagements not reflected in any statement. If it does not appear in
the framework, it cannot inform decisions. The operator runs blind.

**Per locked feedback: "No Patches — Full Fix Only."** Applied to
accounting: if a number is wrong, the source data is fixed and the
statements regenerated. Adjusting one statement to make it tie to
another without fixing the source is the financial equivalent of a
band-aid. The framework only works when the underlying records are
honest.
