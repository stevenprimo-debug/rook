# Finance Fluency — Progression Path

## Who This Is For

The operator who runs their own P&L (or wants to) and refuses to delegate financial literacy to "the accountant." Founders, solo-business owners, agency operators, course creators, and anyone whose decisions are gated by cash, margin, runway, or capital allocation.

By the end of this path you should be able to:
- Read a financial statement cold and trace any line back to source.
- Build a 12-month cash forecast in a spreadsheet without templates or AI assist.
- Model an acquisition or hire under three scenarios with kill criteria named.
- Underwrite a real capital decision in a written investment memo.
- Walk into a bank meeting or board call without flinching.
- Read a competitor's filings and infer their unit economics.

## Stage 1: Foundations (weeks 1-2)

**Goal:** Read the three statements — income statement, balance sheet, cash flow — and know what each line means. Compute personal net worth and burn rate from your own bank/credit card data without help.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Financial Intelligence for Entrepreneurs* — Karen Berman and Joe Knight (Harvard Business Review Press).
- *The Personal MBA* — Josh Kaufman, Part 4 (Money).
- *Accounting Made Simple* — Mike Piper. 100 pages; the fastest competent primer in print.
- *Profit First* — Mike Michalowicz (read after the basics; useful for the allocation discipline).
- Aswath Damodaran's free YouTube series **"Foundations of Finance"** (NYU Stern, full semester posted free). Watch the first six lectures.
- Khan Academy — **"Finance and Capital Markets"** unit. Free, browser-based, with exercises that grade themselves.
- **Investopedia Academy** — the free fundamental-analysis courses cover the same material with quizzes.
- **Bench's free "Accounting Basics for Entrepreneurs"** primer — solid one-evening read.
- **Wall Street Prep's free YouTube tutorials** for spreadsheet-side financial modeling.

**Practice:**
- Download three months of personal bank and credit card statements.
- Build a monthly P&L for yourself in Google Sheets or Excel. No template — formulas hand-typed. Reconcile to the penny.
- Write your own balance sheet: assets minus liabilities equals net worth. Update monthly going forward.
- Compute your personal burn rate and runway in months. If runway is under 12 months, write the three actions that get you to 18.
- Categorize the last 90 days of transactions into fixed / variable / discretionary. Identify the three discretionary categories with the highest spend.
- Build a one-page "personal financial dashboard" you check weekly: cash on hand, net worth, monthly burn, runway months.
- Pull one company's 10-K (Apple, Costco, Buffer — pick one whose business you understand). Read the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement end-to-end.

**Skill check:**
- Hand someone your three statements and have them ask "where did this number come from?" — you can trace every line back to a source row without hesitation.
- You can define EBITDA, gross margin, operating cash flow, working capital, and accruals in one sentence each without looking them up.
- You can read a single line item on a 10-K (e.g., "deferred revenue") and explain why it lives in liabilities and what it implies about the business.

## Stage 2: Applied Practice (weeks 3-6)

**Goal:** Model a business decision under three scenarios. Build a 12-month cash forecast. Audit a SaaS pricing model.

**Read / Watch:**
- *The Outsiders* — William Thorndike. Eight CEOs who allocated capital better than the market for decades. Re-read the Henry Singleton chapter twice.
- *Simple Numbers, Straight Talk, Big Profits* — Greg Crabtree. The most useful operator-side P&L management book published in the last decade.
- *Profit Above All* — Bob Fifer. Old-school but the OpEx discipline carries.
- *Monetizing Innovation* — Madhavan Ramanujam and Georg Tacke. The pricing book most product operators need.
- Patrick O'Shaughnessy's podcast **"Invest Like the Best"** — episodes with capital allocators (Will Thorndike, Pat Dorsey, Howard Marks).
- HBS Online — **"Leading with Finance"** (six-week course, certificate). Worth the money once you have the foundation.
- **CFI (Corporate Finance Institute) free courses** — the financial-modeling fundamentals course is free and competent.
- **ChartMogul's SaaS metrics blog** — for the recurring-revenue financial discipline.
- **The vendored reference clipping** `context/references/mastering-saas-pricing.md` — internalize before any pricing decision.
- **The vendored reference clipping** `context/references/top-15-financial-manager-skills.md` for the skills inventory.

**Practice:**
- Build a 12-month cash forecast for your business or side project. Three columns: bear, base, bull.
- Variables hand-named at the top so anyone can change one input and see the chain react.
- Take one of your current SaaS / tool subscriptions and audit it: cost per use, alternatives, switch cost. Cancel or consolidate at least two.
- Model a hire (or contractor engagement) under three scenarios: hits target, half-target, miss. Compute the breakeven month and the kill date.
- Read three public-company 10-K filings end-to-end (pick companies in your sector). Underline every line you do not understand.
- Build a unit-economics worksheet for one of your offerings: CAC, LTV, gross margin per unit, payback period.
- Run a monthly close discipline: by the 5th of each month, the prior month is closed, reconciled, and dashboarded.
- Develop a 13-week rolling cash forecast — the operating-cash discipline most operators don't run until it's too late.

**Skill check:**
- Hand someone your 12-month forecast and have them stress-test one assumption (e.g., "what if revenue grows half as fast?"). The model recomputes correctly with one cell change. No broken formulas.
- You can defend each major assumption in the forecast with a named source.
- Your unit-economics worksheet shows a payback period under 12 months on at least one offering — or you have a written explanation of why the longer payback is rational.

## Stage 3: Advanced Mastery (months 2-6)

**Goal:** Underwrite a real capital decision — acquisition, equity raise, hire, product line — with a written investment memo. Read a competitor's financials and infer their unit economics. Build a freedom-fund / runway plan with explicit kill criteria.

**Read / Watch:**
- *Damodaran on Valuation* — Aswath Damodaran. The textbook. Pair with his free spreadsheets at pages.stern.nyu.edu/~adamodar/.
- *The Intelligent Investor* — Benjamin Graham, with Jason Zweig commentary. Read for the temperament, not the formulas.
- *Margin of Safety* — Seth Klarman (out of print; PDF circulates legally on Klarman's firm site).
- *The Most Important Thing* — Howard Marks. Pair with his Oaktree memos (free on oaktreecapital.com).
- *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits* — Philip Fisher. The qualitative-investing lineage that complements Graham.
- *One Up on Wall Street* — Peter Lynch.
- Aswath Damodaran's NYU MBA **"Valuation"** course on YouTube. Full semester. Free.
- *Buy Then Build* — Walker Deibel. Acquisition lens for operators who would rather buy than start.
- *The Lifestyle Investor* — Justin Donald. Cash-flow-asset construction for solo operators.
- *The Psychology of Money* — Morgan Housel.
- *Poor Charlie's Almanack* — Charlie Munger / Peter Kaufman. The multi-mental-model frame applied to investing.
- *Berkshire Hathaway annual shareholder letters* (1965-present; free at berkshirehathaway.com). Read at least the last 10.

**Practice:**
- Pick one public competitor in your space. Read their last three years of 10-Ks. Build their unit economics from disclosed metrics. Compare to yours.
- Underwrite one real or hypothetical acquisition / equity raise / major hire. Write a one-page investment memo: thesis, three scenarios, kill criteria, decision.
- Build a "freedom fund" plan: net worth target, monthly contribution, asset allocation, the date by which the rented-income job (or any income source) can be retired.
- Stress test the freedom-fund plan under recession and inflation.
- Walk your business through a tax-projection exercise with a CPA. Understand every line on the return; do not let it be a black box.
- Develop a quarterly capital-allocation review: every quarter, list every place capital is currently allocated. Score each for return and risk. Reallocate as the math dictates.
- Build a downside-survival plan: what does the business look like at 30%, 50%, 70% revenue decline. Name the cuts. Name the threshold at which the business pivots vs. closes.
- Run a personal annual-report exercise: at year-end write a shareholder-style letter to yourself covering capital allocation, returns, mistakes, next-year bets.

**Skill check:**
- A peer operator hands you their P&L and balance sheet cold. Inside 20 minutes you can name the two biggest financial risks, the one allocation move that compounds, and the question you would ask their accountant. No spreadsheet required.
- Your investment memo has been read by a peer or advisor; the decision either shipped or got killed with named reasoning.
- Your downside-survival plan has been tested mentally against an actual scenario (slow quarter, lost customer, recession) and the named cuts felt survivable.

**Quarterly cadence:**
- Review your own books quarterly the same way a public company does. Write a short shareholder letter to yourself.
- Re-run the freedom-fund plan with the latest numbers. Update the target date if reality has changed.
- Audit one new business decision against the "three-scenario / kill-criterion" rubric. Refine the rubric where it broke.
- Compare your performance against one public-company benchmark in your sector.
