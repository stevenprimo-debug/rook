# Customer Truth Discipline

## What This Framework Is

Customer truth discipline is the practice of grounding every
sales decision in what the customer actually said and did —
not in what the sales team hopes is true, assumes is true, or
extrapolates from incomplete signals. The framework holds that
**the most expensive sales failures come from acting on
assumed customer state**: assuming the budget is committed
when it wasn't, assuming the timeline is firm when it wasn't,
assuming the decision-maker is on board when they weren't.

The discipline operates as a recurring question across every
stage: **what do we actually know, vs. what are we assuming?**
Every assumption becomes a verification action. Every
verification becomes pipeline state. Pipeline state that rests
on assumptions instead of customer truth is fragile —
discovered fragile at the worst possible moment (proposal
submission, negotiation, contract).

Five customer-truth dimensions get verified explicitly:

1. **Budget truth** — what does the customer actually have
   approved? Not "they said they have budget" but "where is
   that budget in their system, who approved it, what does
   their procurement process require?"

2. **Authority truth** — who actually decides? The named
   decision-maker may not be the actual one. The procurement
   process may add gates. The technical decision-maker may
   defer to legal, finance, or facilities.

3. **Timeline truth** — what's the real deadline? "We need
   this by Q3" may translate to "we'd like this by Q3 but
   could slip to Q1 next year." Truth is binding events
   (build deliveries, executive moves, facility openings) not
   stated preferences.

4. **Decision-process truth** — how do they actually decide?
   Number of stakeholders, evaluation criteria, competing
   vendors, weight of factors. The decision process drives
   what the proposal must emphasize.

5. **Pain truth** — what's actually broken in their current
   state? Manufactured pain (proposed by salespeople, not
   acknowledged by customer) collapses at decision time.
   Real pain (named by customer with specifics) drives
   purchase decisions.

## Why It Matters For This Agent

Sales Director's Customer-Truth-Pole is the gate that prevents
deals from progressing on assumption rather than verified state.
The pole catches three specific failure modes:

1. **Assumption stacking** — deals where each stage rests on
   the prior stage's unverified assumptions. Proposal stage
   assumes qualified budget; negotiation stage assumes
   approved process; close stage assumes decision-maker buy-in.
   When the deal stalls, the assumption chain unravels.

2. **Hope-based forecasting** — pipeline projections that
   weight deals based on the director's hope rather than
   customer truth. Misses target; produces downstream
   financial planning failures.

3. **Proposal misfit** — proposals that emphasize what the
   sales team believes matters rather than what the customer's
   decision process actually weighs. Loses to competitors who
   tailored to actual customer truth.

produces wins in competitive proposal stages: when [your employer]'s
proposal demonstrates understanding of the client's actual
decision criteria, named decision-makers, real timeline,
and specific named pain — the proposal stands out from
generic competitor proposals that treat the client as a
demographic rather than a specific organization.

## Core Concepts

### 1. The Budget-Truth Verification

Budget truth has specific verification steps:

- **Named approval** — who specifically approved the budget?
  CFO sign-off? Capex committee? Board approval?
- **Budget code / line item** — where does this project live
  in their financial system? "General facilities budget"
  is vague; "Project N-2027-04 line 7" is specific.
- **Fiscal year alignment** — does the budget match the
  fiscal year? Q4 spend on a Q3-fiscal budget can stall.
- **Procurement requirements** — single-source justification?
  Three competing bids required? Pre-approved vendor list?
- **Payment terms** — net 30, 60, 90? Milestone billing?
  Final-payment-on-acceptance?

Each verification step is a question to the prospect, not an
assumption. "Could you walk me through the budget approval
process for this project?" — the answer is data.

### 2. The Authority-Truth Mapping

The decision-maker mapping has multiple layers:

- **Stated decision-maker** — whoever introduced themselves
  in that role.
- **Technical decision-maker** — the engineering/facilities
  lead who evaluates fit.
- **Economic decision-maker** — whoever holds the budget
  authority.
- **User decision-maker** — whoever will operate the system.
- **Influencer / blocker** — other stakeholders whose
  objections can stall the decision.
- **Procurement / Legal / Finance** — gate-keepers whose
  process requirements can stall or restructure deals.

For [your employer] deals at enterprise scale, all six layers exist.
Discovering one of them late in the process produces stalls.

The mapping is built through asking explicitly: "Who else is
involved in the decision? What does the procurement process
look like? Who has to approve the final agreement?"

### 3. The Timeline-Truth Anchoring

Timelines anchor to binding external events:

- **Facility opening dates** — when the building opens, when
  the renovation completes.
- **Executive announcements** — town-hall events, board
  meetings, public launches.
- **Fiscal-year boundaries** — capex must spend by year-end,
  budget rolls forward, etc.
- **Lease events** — move-in dates, lease expiration,
  consolidation timelines.
- **Competing project dependencies** — IT migration must
  complete first, network upgrade must precede, etc.

Anchored timelines are firm. Unanchored timelines ("we'd like
this in Q3") are preferences that can slip 6-12 months.

The agent surfaces anchors by asking: "What event drives this
timeline? What happens if this slips?" The answer reveals
the binding event or the absence of one.

### 4. The Decision-Process Discovery

How a customer actually decides drives proposal emphasis:

- **Single decision-maker** — proposal targeted to that
  person's priorities.
- **Committee with weighted criteria** — proposal addresses
  each criterion in order of weight.
- **Procurement-led** — proposal emphasizes terms, references,
  compliance.
- **CFO-led** — proposal emphasizes ROI, payback, financial
  justification.
- **Technical-led** — proposal emphasizes capability match,
  engineering depth.
- **Risk-averse** — proposal emphasizes references, similar
  successful projects, risk mitigation.

The decision process is the proposal architect. The wrong
proposal for the right decision process loses to the right
proposal that may be technically inferior.

The agent asks: "Walk me through how a project like this
typically gets evaluated and approved at your organization."

### 5. The Pain-Truth Test

Real pain has specific markers:

- **Named in customer's words** — the customer used their own
  language, not the salesperson's.
- **Specific instance** — a concrete event when the pain
  showed up (not "we have issues sometimes" but "last quarter
  the conference room AV failed mid-board meeting").
- **Quantified cost** — the pain has a dollar, time, or
  reputation cost the customer can name.
- **Owner of the pain** — someone specific cares about this
  pain; it's not abstract organizational concern.

Manufactured pain ("you must be having trouble with X") fails
the test. The customer hasn't named the pain, doesn't have a
specific instance, doesn't have a quantified cost, doesn't
have a named owner. Proposals built on manufactured pain
collapse at decision time because the pain doesn't drive
decision.

### 6. The Note Discipline

Customer truth lives in notes, not in memory. The discipline:

- **CRM notes per interaction** — date, attendees, specific
  statements made, specific commitments made (by either side).
- **Verbatim quotes when stakes are high** — the customer's
  exact words on budget, timeline, decision process.
- **Action items with owners** — every interaction ends with
  named follow-ups.
- **Updated state per interaction** — what changed in the
  customer's situation since last touch.

Per locked memory pattern (compounding-append), customer truth
accumulates across interactions. The director loads the prior
notes before each interaction, identifies what's still
unknown, plans the questions that close the gaps.

### 7. The Verification Loop

Customer truth is verified, not assumed. The verification loop:

1. **Identify the assumption** — "I'm assuming the CFO has
   approved the budget."
2. **Plan the verification** — "Next call, I'll ask: 'How did
   the budget approval go through? Who signed off?'"
3. **Execute the verification** — the call surfaces the answer.
4. **Update state** — the assumption becomes verified truth
   (or gets corrected).

The loop runs continuously. Every stage has verifications
that gate progression. Deals that progress without verification
ride on assumptions that may collapse.

## Common Applications

**Qualification call template:**
The agent prepares qualification call agendas that systematically
verify each of the five truth dimensions. Questions are open-ended,
designed to surface customer truth in their words.

**Pre-proposal customer-truth audit:**
Before producing a proposal, the agent runs the audit. Are budget
truth, authority truth, timeline truth, decision-process truth,
and pain truth all verified? Gaps get filled with a follow-up
discovery call. Proposals built on verified truth win at higher
rates than proposals built on assumptions.

**Stalled-deal diagnostic:**
A deal stalls. The agent runs the diagnostic: which truth
dimensions were assumed rather than verified? Often the answer
reveals the actual blocker — a stakeholder we didn't know about,
a budget process we didn't understand, a timeline that wasn't
real.

**Pipeline forecast scrub:**
For each pipeline opportunity, the agent surfaces which truths
are verified and which are assumed. Opportunities with high
assumption-content get probability adjusted down; opportunities
with high verification-content get probability adjusted up. The
honest aggregate matches likely close.

**Per locked feedback: "Don't Infer Client Entity Names."**
Customer truth includes using the exact names the customer uses.
Inferred names produce errors that erode trust.

**Per locked feedback: "Verify Project Status Before Speaking."**
Customer truth requires verification before claims. "The CFO
approved the budget last month" is verified or assumed; the
distinction matters.

**Per locked memory: Pipeline snapshot location.** Customer-truth
notes per opportunity live in the project folder; pipeline state
references the notes for source.

## Anti-patterns (when this framework is misapplied)

**Assumption stacking.** Each stage rests on the prior stage's
unverified assumptions. Discovered fragile at the worst moment.

**Hope-based forecasting.** Forecasts weight deals by the
director's hope rather than verified customer truth. Misses
target.

**Manufactured pain.** Proposals built on pain the customer
hasn't named with specifics. Collapses at decision time.

**Per locked feedback: "Don't Infer Client Entity Names."**
Inferred customer language erodes trust; verified language
demonstrates understanding.

**Per locked feedback: "Verify Project Status Before Speaking."**
Customer-state claims must be verified. Speaking from memory of
last week's state produces stale claims.

**Per locked feedback: "Brand to the customer's trade."**
Customer truth includes their trade context. Generic
"transformative experiences" framing fails; specific operator-
trade language wins.

**Per locked feedback: "Investigate Before Apologizing for
Perceived Bugs."** When a customer signal seems negative,
investigate what's actually happening before assuming you
misread the situation. The customer may be testing, may be
processing, may be working through internal politics — all
require different responses.

**Note neglect.** Customer truth left in director's memory
rather than written notes. Accumulating context, but
loss-prone and not transferable.

**Verbal commitments treated as written.** "They said they'd
sign" is not "they signed." Written verification (email, MSA,
PO) is the truth; verbal is a precursor.

**Per locked feedback: "Numbers-Honest-Pole" (cross-pole with
Finance Manager).** Customer truth feeds financial truth.
Aspirational customer state produces aspirational financial
forecasts.

**Skipping discovery to accelerate.** Rushing through
qualification to "get to the proposal faster." The proposal
quality depends on customer truth quality; rushed truth
produces misfit proposals.
