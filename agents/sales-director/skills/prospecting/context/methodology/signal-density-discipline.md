# Signal Density Discipline

## What This Framework Is

Signal density discipline is the practice of evaluating prospects
not just on static fit (does this organization match the ICP?)
but on how much active buying signal is firing around them right
now. The framework holds that **timing multiplies fit**: a
moderate-fit prospect with strong active signals outperforms a
high-fit prospect with no active signals, because the signaling
prospect is more likely to engage at this moment.

The discipline operates on the recognition that B2B buying
decisions correlate with **trigger events** — specific external
changes that create momentum toward purchase decisions. Buying
decisions rarely happen in static states; they happen when
something has changed (new executive, new construction, new
budget cycle, new strategic priority) that requires action.

Signal density is the count and recency of trigger events at a
prospect organization. High density means multiple recent
triggers compounding; low density means a static state with no
active buying momentum.

Five trigger categories drive signal density:

1. **Executive triggers** — new VP/Director hires, executive
   departures, role consolidations or expansions.
2. **Facility triggers** — new construction, renovation,
   relocation, expansion, lease events.
3. **Strategic triggers** — RTO mandates, acquisition activity,
   IPO/exit announcements, restructures, strategic refocuses.
4. **Financial triggers** — earnings call mentions of relevant
   investment, capital allocation announcements, budget
   reset.
5. **Public-facing triggers** — press releases, conference
   talks, podcast appearances, public statements by named
   executives.

Each category surfaces through different sources; high-density
prospects show multiple categories firing simultaneously.

## Why It Matters For This Agent

Prospecting Agent's Signal-Density-Pole gates pipeline entry
on the timing-fit combination, not fit alone. The pole catches
three specific failure modes:

1. **Fit-only prospecting** — perfectly-targeted prospects with
   no active signals. Outreach lands on quiet prospects who
   acknowledge interest but don't move forward because no
   triggering event creates urgency.

2. **Signal-only prospecting** — prospects with active signals
   but poor fit. Outreach lands on prospects who engage but
   produce below-threshold opportunities.

3. **Signal-blind timing** — high-fit prospects whose triggers
   fired 12+ months ago. The window has closed; outreach is
   late.

For the operator's [your employer] outreach pipeline, signal density discipline
multiplies the per-message response rate. Per the locked playbook
math: trigger-event-driven outreach produces 3-5x the response
rate of cold outreach without triggers. Signal density discipline
is what captures the multiplier.

## Core Concepts

### 1. The Trigger-Event Catalog

Each trigger category has specific event types:

**Executive triggers:**
- New VP Workplace Tech / Dir Facilities / Dir Corp Real
  Estate / CTO / CIO hire at named org.
- Departure of incumbent (often signals strategy reset).
- C-Suite reshuffle that affects facility/IT priorities.
- Public announcement by named executive of relevant strategy.

**Facility triggers:**
- New HQ construction announced or broken ground.
- Major renovation announced.
- Office consolidation / relocation.
- Lease renewal or expiration approaching (3-12 month window).
- Anchor tenant lease at new building.
- Acquisition of a new facility.

**Strategic triggers:**
- RTO mandate announcement (drives town hall / experiential
  space investment).
- Acquisition (drives integration of new spaces).
- IPO or major funding event (drives capital allocation
  including facility upgrade).
- Restructure with named facility implications.
- Strategic refocus toward customer experience, employee
  experience, or workplace transformation.

**Financial triggers:**
- Earnings call mention of facility investment or
  workplace transformation.
- Capital allocation announcement including AV or facility
  modernization.
- Budget reset or new fiscal-year capex announcement.
- Public RFP for AV / experiential / integration scope.

**Public-facing triggers:**
- Press release mentioning relevant capability gap or
  upgrade.
- Conference talk by named executive on workplace strategy.
- Podcast or interview surfacing facility pain points.
- Public LinkedIn post by named target about relevant
  topic.

### 2. The Recency Decay

Trigger relevance decays with time:

- **0-30 days**: peak signal density. Outreach within this
  window has highest response likelihood.
- **30-90 days**: strong signal. Outreach still highly
  effective.
- **90-180 days**: moderate signal. Outreach acceptable but
  competing with prospect attention.
- **180-365 days**: weak signal. Outreach should reference the
  trigger but not lead with it.
- **365+ days**: signal-stale. Decision likely made; outreach
  approaches as cold without trigger.

The decay curve means trigger monitoring is real-time, not
quarterly. The 0-30 day window is where signal density compounds
into response rates.

### 3. The Multi-Trigger Compounding

A single trigger is good; multiple compounding triggers are
better:

- **One trigger (e.g., new construction)**: moderate signal density.
  Outreach approaches the construction theme.
- **Two compounding triggers (new construction + executive
  hire)**: high signal density. The new executive often drives
  decisions on the new construction. Outreach can reference
  both, producing a stronger relevance signal.
- **Three+ compounding triggers**: peak signal density.
  Strong probability the prospect is actively evaluating
  vendors.

The compounding effect means signal-density scoring is
multiplicative, not additive. Two triggers don't produce 2x; they
produce closer to 3-4x because they reinforce each other.

### 4. The Source-Aware Monitoring

Different trigger categories surface through different sources:

- **Executive triggers**: LinkedIn job-change tracking,
  industry press, company press releases.
- **Facility triggers**: construction permit databases, building
  press, commercial real estate publications, named announcement
  channels.
- **Strategic triggers**: earnings calls, press releases,
  industry trade press.
- **Financial triggers**: SEC filings (for public companies),
  earnings calls, capital-allocation announcements.
- **Public-facing triggers**: PR newsfeeds, LinkedIn monitoring,
  podcast monitoring.

The agent maintains source-aware monitoring across these
channels. [your prospecting tool] (per locked MCP) surfaces some triggers
directly; other categories require monitoring beyond [your prospecting tool].

### 5. The Signal-Density Scoring

Each prospect gets a signal-density score:

- **5/5**: 3+ active triggers (within 90 days) with multiple
  categories.
- **4/5**: 2 active triggers with category diversity.
- **3/5**: 1 strong active trigger (within 30 days).
- **2/5**: 1 trigger present but older (90-180 days).
- **1/5**: trigger stale (180+ days) or no specific trigger.

The signal-density score multiplies with the ICP-fit score (per
companion methodology). Composite = ICP-fit × signal-density
weight. The combined score drives pipeline priority.

### 6. The "Why Now" Question

Every outreach references "why now" — what's making this an
appropriate moment to engage. The signal density discipline
provides the answer:

- "Your new HQ broke ground last month..."
- "I saw the announcement about the new Chief Workplace
  Officer..."
- "The Q3 earnings call mentioned the experiential customer
  center investment..."

The "why now" is the prospect-facing manifestation of signal
density. Outreach without a "why now" defaults to generic
relevance, which is much weaker than trigger-event relevance.

### 7. The Pipeline-Refresh Cycle

Signal density doesn't sit static. Prospects move between
density tiers as triggers fire and decay:

- **New triggers fire**: previously-cold prospects rise to
  active pipeline status.
- **Triggers decay**: high-density prospects who didn't engage
  fall back to lower priority.
- **New prospects emerge**: the wide-net ICP includes prospects
  not previously in the pipeline; new triggers surface them.

The pipeline refresh cycle is monthly or more frequent for hot
verticals. Per locked locked compounding-append memory pattern,
the refresh produces an audit trail of which triggers
preceded which deals.

## Common Applications

**Trigger-event scan for the week:**
The agent runs a multi-source scan: [your prospecting tool] for executive
changes, construction permit databases for facility events,
earnings call transcripts for strategic signals. Output: list of
trigger events at named organizations, with date, category, and
prospect mapping.

**Signal-density scoring on a candidate prospect:**
A new candidate enters the pool with ICP-fit score of 18/25.
The agent checks signal density: new VP Workplace Tech hire 6
weeks ago + RTO mandate announcement 3 weeks ago + earnings
mention of customer experience investment last quarter. Three
compounding triggers within 90 days = 5/5 signal density.
Combined: high-priority T1 outreach.

**Cold-prospect deprioritization:**
A previously-high-fit prospect has had no trigger events in
180+ days. Signal density drops to 1/5. The agent deprioritizes
outreach; if surrounded by stronger signal-density prospects
in the queue, this one gets parked with a re-check trigger
("re-evaluate when next executive announcement fires").

**Trigger-event timed outreach handoff:**
The agent surfaces a high signal density prospect to the
sales-outreach agent. Includes: ICP-fit score, signal-density
score, named triggers with dates, recommended "why now"
framing for the message. Outreach agent produces the specific
.eml file referencing the named triggers.

**Source-monitoring health audit:**
The agent reviews source monitoring across the trigger
categories. Any sources that haven't surfaced new triggers in
the expected interval get audited — maybe the source broke,
maybe the monitoring scope is too narrow.

## Anti-patterns (when this framework is misapplied)

**Signal-blind prospecting.** Pursuing well-fit prospects
without checking signal density. Outreach lands at quiet
moments; response rates drop; pipeline fills with prospects
who won't move in the relevant timeframe.

**Trigger-only prospecting.** Pursuing every prospect with
recent triggers regardless of fit. Outreach response rates
look acceptable, but the resulting opportunities don't pass
auto-reject thresholds — activity without revenue.

**Old-trigger reliance.** Referencing triggers that fired 12+
months ago. The prospect has likely already made the decision
or moved past the moment. Reference is stale.

**Single-source dependence.** Relying only on [your prospecting tool] or only
on press releases for trigger detection. Each source covers
some categories well and others poorly. Multi-source monitoring
is required.

**Per locked feedback: "Verify Project Status Before Speaking."**
Trigger data must reflect actual current state. Triggers
identified from outdated sources produce false signals.

**Trigger fabrication.** Inventing relevance ("I noticed you
might be considering...") when no actual trigger fired. The
prospect detects the fabrication and disengages.

**Per locked feedback: "Don't Infer Client Entity Names."**
Trigger references use the exact names the organization uses
for facilities, roles, events. Inferred or paraphrased names
weaken the trigger reference.

**Refresh-cycle neglect.** Allowing the pipeline to drift
without refresh cycles. Prospects whose signal density has
risen get missed; prospects whose density has decayed remain
in active outreach.

**Trigger compounding ignored.** Treating each trigger as
isolated. A new executive + new construction + RTO mandate at
the same org is 3x stronger than a single trigger; treating
them separately produces less effective outreach.

**Per locked feedback: "Filter — Personal-Tool Patterns vs
Agent-Team Patterns."** Trigger data must be team-readable.
Outreach agent and sales director both need to load trigger
context; personal-shorthand tagging fails.

**Conference-talk monitoring overlooked.** Many high-value
triggers surface through executive conference talks and
podcast appearances. Monitoring limited to press releases
misses these.
