# deep-researcher — Reference Pack

> Curated authoritative sources for multi-step research, source synthesis, and confidence calibration. For use with the the Stack deep-researcher agent.

---

## Source 1: Superforecasting — Good Judgment Project (Philip Tetlock)

**Author/Publisher:** Philip E. Tetlock & Barbara Mellers (co-founders), Good Judgment Inc. / University of Pennsylvania; technical white paper by Christopher W. Karvetski, PhD
**URL (methodology hub):** https://goodjudgment.com/superforecasting-explained-in-podcasts-and-videos/
**URL (empirical paper — Brier scores):** https://goodjudgment.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/10/Superforecasters-A-Decade-of-Stochastic-Dominance.pdf
**Domain teaching:** Probabilistic confidence calibration — Brier scoring, calibration vs. resolution, belief-updating (Bayesian incremental revision), outside-view base-rate anchoring, and the empirical gap between hedgehog experts and calibrated generalist forecasters.
**Why authoritative:** Tetlock's Good Judgment Project ran the US Intelligence Advanced Research Projects Activity (IARPA) forecasting tournament 2011–2015, in which Superforecasters outperformed professional intelligence analysts with classified access by over 30%. The Karvetski white paper (2021) provides six years of post-tournament Brier score data, showing Superforecasters achieve a 79% improvement in miscalibration over crowd forecasters. This is the primary empirical source for operationalising confidence calibration — not a summary or commentary.

## Source 2: Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Version 6.5, 2024)

**Author/Publisher:** Julian Higgins & James Thomas (Senior Editors) et al.; Cochrane Collaboration (international independent evidence-synthesis body)
**URL:** https://www.cochrane.org/authors/handbooks-and-manuals/handbook/current
**Domain teaching:** Systematic evidence synthesis methodology — pre-specified question formulation (PICO), exhaustive multi-database search strategy, risk-of-bias assessment (RoB 2 tool), GRADE framework for certainty-of-evidence grading, meta-analysis methods, and the structured prevention of confirmation bias through pre-registered protocols.
**Why authoritative:** The Cochrane Handbook is the globally recognised gold standard for systematic review methodology, cited in thousands of peer-reviewed journals and adopted as mandatory guidance by the Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews (CDSR). Version 6.5 (August 2024) is the most current edition, continuously maintained by methodologists at leading research universities. It directly addresses the multi-source synthesis problem: how to combine studies without cherry-picking, how to quantify uncertainty, and how to distinguish signal from noise in a body of evidence.

## Source 3: PRISMA 2020 Statement — Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses

**Author/Publisher:** Matthew J. Page, Joanne E. McKenzie, Patrick M. Bossuyt, Isabelle Boutron, Tammy C. Hoffmann, Cynthia D. Mulrow, et al. Published simultaneously in *BMJ* (2021;372:n71, doi: 10.1136/bmj.n71) and four other peer-reviewed journals.
**URL:** https://www.prisma-statement.org/prisma-2020-statement
**Domain teaching:** Research-report structure and inline citation discipline — the 27-item PRISMA checklist codifies how a multi-source synthesis must be reported: rationale, eligibility criteria, search strategy, selection process, risk-of-bias assessment, synthesis methods, certainty-of-evidence, and a PRISMA flow diagram mapping every inclusion/exclusion decision.
**Why authoritative:** PRISMA 2020 is the peer-reviewed reporting standard endorsed by hundreds of journals worldwide (including *BMJ*, *JAMA*, *The Lancet*, and *PLOS Medicine*) and by the EQUATOR Network (the international body for health-research reporting transparency). It is not a methodology guide but a mandatory transparency checklist — meaning it defines the minimum citation and reporting discipline expected of any credible evidence synthesis. Published 2021; current.

## Source 4: "Lateral Reading: Reading Less and Learning More When Evaluating Digital Information"

**Author/Publisher:** Sam Wineburg & Sarah McGrew, Stanford History Education Group (SHEG), Stanford University. Working paper SSRN 3048994 (posted 2017, last revised January 2025); shortened version in *Teachers College Record*, 121, Nov. 2019.
**URL:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3048994
**Domain teaching:** Source-credibility evaluation at speed — the empirical discovery that professional fact-checkers assess source credibility by *leaving* a page immediately and reading laterally (opening new tabs to see what others say about the source), rather than reading vertically (spending time on the source itself). Defines primary vs. secondary credibility signals and why domain-name, logo, and "About" page cues are easily gamed.
**Why authoritative:** This is the original peer-reviewed research paper establishing lateral reading as a superior credibility-evaluation strategy. The study used a controlled experiment with three groups (PhD historians, professional fact-checkers, Stanford undergraduates), finding fact-checkers reached more accurate credibility judgments in a fraction of the time. The paper directly answers the primary-vs.-secondary source hierarchy question with empirical evidence rather than prescription. It has been replicated and built upon in subsequent Stanford/SHEG Civic Online Reasoning curriculum studies.

## Source 5: "SIFT (The Four Moves)" — Mike Caulfield

**Author/Publisher:** Michael Caulfield, digital literacy researcher (then at Washington State University; later at the University of Washington Center for an Informed Public). Published 2019, licensed CC BY 4.0.
**URL:** https://hapgood.us/2019/06/19/sift-the-four-moves/
**Domain teaching:** Practical source-credibility workflow — the four-move decision framework: **S**top (pause before reading or sharing), **I**nvestigate the source (lateral reading before deep engagement), **F**ind better coverage (trade up to a higher-quality source on the same claim), **T**race claims, quotes, and media back to original context (upstream sourcing). Operationalises the Wineburg/McGrew lateral reading finding into a repeatable agent-facing procedure.
**Why authoritative:** Caulfield is the practitioner who translated the SHEG empirical research into an actionable workflow. SIFT is now embedded in curricula at hundreds of universities and is the method endorsed by the Stanford History Education Group's Civic Online Reasoning program. The CC BY 4.0 licence makes it the reference that downstream adaptations (including this pack's own domain) must cite. It is the closest thing to an industry-standard source-evaluation SOP for web research, with documented adoption across journalism, library science, and intelligence tradecraft communities.

## Source 6: "A Guide for Evaluating Sources" — Poynter

**Author/Publisher:** Joann Byrd, The Poynter Institute for Media Studies (professional journalism training institute, St. Petersburg, Florida). Published 2002.
**URL:** https://www.poynter.org/reporting-editing/2002/a-guide-for-evaluating-sources/
**Domain teaching:** Journalist-facing primary-source hierarchy — structured questions reporters and editors must apply at every sourcing decision: question the source's motives and agenda, question the reporter-source relationship and bias, question reliability and proximity to the story, question assumptions and what viewpoints are missing. Establishes the "named source backed by named backup" standard and the gatekeeping chain of custody for information.
**Why authoritative:** The Poynter Institute is the primary professional education body for American journalism and trains reporters at hundreds of newsrooms. This guide is a canonical practitioner document (not an academic paper) that represents the applied operationalisation of primary-source discipline in journalism — a distinct and complementary framework to the library-science and academic-research traditions covered by the other sources. Its 2002 date is permitted under the pack's own quality bar as a canonical foundational text that predates the current AI-generated-content era and has not been superseded.

## Recommended Reading Order

1. **Source 4 — Wineburg & McGrew, "Lateral Reading"** — start here because it provides the empirical foundation for *why* source hierarchy matters and shows that the common intuition (read deeply, check credentials on-page) is measurably worse than leaving the page immediately. Resets the default assumption.

2. **Source 5 — Caulfield, "SIFT (The Four Moves)"** — then this for the practitioner workflow that operationalises Source 4 into a four-step decision procedure the agent can execute on every source it encounters during a research task.

3. **Source 6 — Byrd, Poynter "A Guide for Evaluating Sources"** — then this for the journalist's adversarial lens: motive, relationship, and gatekeeping questions that SIFT does not cover. Establishes the habit of asking *who benefits* from a source's claim.

4. **Source 2 — Cochrane Handbook (Chapter 9: Synthesis; Chapter 7: Bias)** — then this for the full systematic synthesis protocol: how to combine multiple sources without confirmation bias, how to pre-specify questions, and how to use GRADE to express evidence uncertainty. The core methodology for multi-source synthesis.

5. **Source 3 — PRISMA 2020 Statement** — then this for the reporting discipline layer: the 27-item checklist tells you exactly what must be documented, cited, and disclosed when presenting a research synthesis. Apply as a completeness check on any research report.

6. **Source 1 — Good Judgment Project / Superforecasting (Tetlock)** — finish here for the confidence calibration layer: Brier scores, outside-view base rates, Bayesian incremental updating, and the empirical benchmark for what "well-calibrated" actually means numerically. Use to design the agent's uncertainty-expression vocabulary.
